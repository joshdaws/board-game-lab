## Core Design Insights

### 1. Hand Management and Variable Player Powers are near-universal

17 of 25 games use each. These are not differentiators -- they are table stakes. A hand of cards that players must manage, combined with asymmetric starting conditions or abilities, forms the baseline expectation for a top-tier game.

### 2. Solo mode is essentially expected

15 of 25 games support solo play. This has shifted from a nice-to-have to a market expectation at the top tier. Games without solo modes (Twilight Struggle, 7 Wonders Duel, Twilight Imperium, etc.) tend to be explicitly multiplayer-dependent by design.

### 3. Income mechanics are core to sustained engagement

13 games feature income -- recurring resource generation that creates a growth curve. This keeps players invested across long sessions by rewarding earlier decisions with compounding returns.

### 4. Spatial replayability drives replay value

Hexagon Grid (9) and Modular Board (9) appear frequently together. Variable board states ensure no two games play identically, which is critical for games at this weight level where players invest significant time per session.

### 5. Classic mechanics endure alongside modern ones

Dice Rolling (9) and Set Collection (9) remain highly represented despite being among the oldest mechanics in the hobby. Their longevity suggests they solve fundamental design problems (controlled randomness, satisfying collection goals) that newer mechanics complement but do not replace.

### 6. Deckbuilding is established infrastructure

8 games use Deck, Bag, and Pool Building. What began as a genre-defining mechanic (Dominion, 2008) is now a subsystem embedded within larger designs -- Gloomhaven, Dune Imperium, Great Western Trail all use it as one layer among many.

### 7. End Game Bonuses create multiple paths to victory

8 games use End Game Bonuses, providing hidden or delayed scoring that rewards long-term planning and enables diverse strategies within the same game. This mechanic combats the problem of runaway leaders.

### 8. Cooperative games are a major segment

8 of 25 games are cooperative. The co-op segment is not a niche -- it is a pillar. Games like Gloomhaven, Spirit Island, and Pandemic Legacy demonstrate that cooperative play can support the same mechanical depth as competitive games.

### 9. Science Fiction dominates, Fantasy follows

Science Fiction appears in 11 games, Fantasy in 9. Together they account for the vast majority. Economic (8) is the leading non-genre theme, suggesting that systems-driven themes pair well with heavy mechanics.

### 10. The medium-heavy band is where greatness clusters

10 of 25 games fall in the 3.5-4.0 weight range. This suggests an optimal complexity zone: enough depth for meaningful decisions and replayability, but not so heavy that accessibility collapses. Games above 4.0 succeed but are niche; games below 3.0 struggle to rank at the very top.

## Mechanic Combinations Worth Studying

These pairings appear repeatedly in the top 25 and may represent proven synergies:

- **Hand Management + Variable Player Powers** -- the baseline combo
- **Income + Tech Trees** -- engine building with progression
- **Hexagon Grid + Modular Board** -- spatial replayability
- **Cooperative Game + Hand Management + Communication Limits** -- constrained co-op (Gloomhaven, Frosthaven)
- **Deck Building + Worker Placement** -- hybrid engine (Dune Imperium series)
- **Legacy Game + Cooperative Game** -- narrative co-op campaigns
- **Campaign Card Driven + Simultaneous Action Selection** -- tactical combat systems
