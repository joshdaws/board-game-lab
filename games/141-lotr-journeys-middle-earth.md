## Design Notes

Designer: Nathan I. Hajek & Grace Holdinghaus. Published by Fantasy Flight Games (2019).

### Theme Integration

Journeys in Middle-Earth channels Tolkien's emphasis on the journey itself ---
the long road through uncertain terrain, the fellowship tested by hardship, the
quiet moments of preparation before sudden violence. The companion app generates
narrative scenarios that unfold across procedurally revealed tile maps, modeling
the experience of traveling through Middle-earth without foreknowledge of what
lies ahead. Players do not see the full map at the start of a scenario; tiles
are added as heroes explore, creating a literal fog of war that mirrors the
uncertainty Frodo and Aragorn faced in the source material. The app handles
enemy behavior, story branching, and terrain effects, which means the game's
narrative complexity exceeds what a purely cardboard system could manage while
keeping the rules burden on players remarkably low for a campaign game.

Each hero --- Aragorn, Legolas, Gimli, Elena, Beravor, Bilbo --- carries a
personal deck of skill cards that defines their capabilities. This is not a
hand of abstract resources; each card has a name, an illustration, and a
thematic ability tied to that character's identity. Aragorn's cards emphasize
leadership and resilience; Legolas favors ranged precision and elven agility.
When a hero attempts a skill test, they draw cards from their deck and count
successes, making the deck itself a probabilistic engine that the player has
shaped through campaign progression. The deck-as-character concept means that
improving your hero over a campaign is not a matter of incrementing statistics
on a character sheet but of adding, removing, and upgrading cards that change
the probability distributions of your future tests. This is deckbuilding
reimagined as character development.

The role system layers a second axis of customization on top of each hero's
base identity. A hero selects a role --- Captain, Guardian, Hunter, Pathfinder,
Musician, Burglar --- that adds role-specific cards to their deck and grants
unique abilities. The same hero with different roles plays distinctly: Aragorn
as Guardian absorbs damage for allies through defensive card abilities, while
Aragorn as Captain grants action economy bonuses that accelerate the party's
tempo. Roles can change between scenarios within a campaign, allowing the
fellowship to adapt its composition to anticipated challenges without locking
players into rigid character builds.

### Core Loop

Each scenario follows a consistent structure managed by the companion app:

1. RALLY PHASE --- At the start of each round, heroes prepare for action.
   Each hero draws cards from their personal skill deck up to their hand
   limit. Heroes may also scout --- revealing the top cards of their deck
   and choosing to prepare some face-down as reserves for upcoming skill
   tests. Scouting is the game's primary moment of strategic planning:
   preparing strong cards increases test reliability but removes them from
   the draw pool, while leaving weak cards on top risks failed tests later.

2. ACTION PHASE --- In any order the group chooses, each hero takes up to
   two actions from the following: Travel (move to an adjacent tile or
   explore to reveal a new tile), Interact (engage with tokens on the map
   as directed by the app), Attack (engage an enemy in your space using
   cards from hand), and Sprint (move twice but forfeit your second action).
   Actions can be taken in any combination and heroes can act in any order,
   enabling coordinated party tactics.

3. SKILL TESTS --- When an action requires a test (attacking, exploring
   dangerous terrain, interacting with obstacles), the hero draws cards
   from the top of their deck equal to their tested stat and counts success
   icons. Prepared cards (face-down from scouting) can be revealed to add
   guaranteed successes. The number of successes determines the outcome,
   with thresholds set by the app.

4. SHADOW PHASE --- After all heroes have acted, the app takes control.
   Enemies activate according to their AI scripts --- moving toward heroes,
   attacking, or triggering special abilities. Threat increases on a global
   timer, and narrative events may trigger based on scenario conditions.
   The app then prompts for any end-of-round resolution.

5. CAMPAIGN PROGRESSION --- Between scenarios, heroes may spend experience
   to upgrade skill cards (replacing weaker versions with stronger ones),
   acquire new items that add cards to their decks, and select or change
   roles. The campaign map presents branching paths that determine which
   scenarios are available next.

### Key Design Patterns

SKILL DECK AS PROBABILISTIC CHARACTER: The most significant design innovation
in Journeys is replacing dice with a personal skill deck for all tests. Each
hero's deck contains cards with varying numbers of success icons, and drawing
from the deck to resolve tests means that a hero's capabilities are literally
the cards they carry. Deckbuilding during campaign progression does not simply
add power; it reshapes probability curves. Adding a card with two successes
makes strong draws more likely but also dilutes the deck, potentially making
weak draws persist longer between shuffles. Removing a weak card concentrates
the deck toward reliability. This probabilistic character system gives players
meaningful agency over their hero's growth trajectory while preserving
uncertainty in individual tests.

SCOUTING AS INFORMATION MANAGEMENT: The scout action during the Rally Phase
is the game's core strategic decision. Scouting reveals top-of-deck cards and
allows preparing them face-down for guaranteed success in future tests. This
converts uncertainty into reliability at a cost: prepared cards are removed
from the draw pool, meaning fewer cards are available for random draws during
the action phase. A heavily scouted hero has reliable key tests but a thinner
deck for unexpected challenges. A lightly scouted hero retains a full draw
pool but faces more variance. The scouting mechanism gives players meaningful
control over risk management without eliminating the tension of uncertain
outcomes.

APP-MANAGED COMPLEXITY: The companion app absorbs what would otherwise be
enormous rules overhead --- enemy AI, scenario setup, narrative branching, map
generation, loot tables, and campaign state tracking. This allows Journeys to
achieve campaign-game depth at a weight of 2.70, which would be impossible if
players had to manage these systems manually. The design philosophy is that
the app handles bookkeeping and narrative complexity while players focus on
tactical decisions and cooperative strategy. This division of labor between
digital and physical is the game's central architectural decision.

PROCEDURAL MAP REVELATION: Tiles are added to the map as heroes explore,
meaning the full scenario layout is unknown at the start. This procedural
revelation creates genuine exploration --- players must decide whether to push
into unknown territory or consolidate known positions. It also prevents
scenario memorization across replays, since tile placement and token
distribution vary between sessions. The modular tile system means the same
scenario feels different each time, extending campaign replayability.

COOPERATIVE ACTION ORDERING AS TACTICAL PUZZLE: Heroes can act in any order
during the action phase and can interleave their actions freely. This creates
a rich cooperative planning space: a Guardian might move into position to
absorb an attack before the Hunter strikes from range, or a Pathfinder might
explore a tile to reveal enemies before the Captain coordinates the party's
assault. The free action ordering transforms what could be a simple
simultaneous-action system into a collaborative puzzle where sequencing
decisions matter as much as individual action choices.

ROLE SYSTEM AS MODULAR CUSTOMIZATION: Roles add cards to a hero's deck and
grant persistent abilities, but they operate independently from the hero's
base identity. This two-axis customization (hero plus role) creates
combinatorial variety without requiring dozens of unique characters. Five
heroes with six roles yield thirty distinct configurations, each playing
differently because the role cards interact with the hero's base deck in
unique ways. The ability to change roles between scenarios provides campaign
flexibility without permanent commitment.

### What Makes It #141

Journeys in Middle-Earth solved the fundamental problem of cooperative campaign
games: how to deliver narrative depth and tactical variety without burying
players under rules. The companion app absorbs the complexity that would
otherwise require a dedicated game master or dozens of pages of scenario
scripting, while the skill deck system replaces dice with a character-building
mechanism that gives players meaningful agency over their hero's probability
curves. At weight 2.70, it is remarkably accessible for a game that delivers
multi-session campaigns with branching narratives, persistent character
progression, and procedurally generated maps. The Tolkien license does real
thematic work --- the journey-focused scenario design, the fellowship dynamics
of cooperative action ordering, and the character archetypes all draw directly
from the source material rather than treating the IP as a marketing wrapper.
The result is a game that captures the feel of a Tolkien quest more
successfully than many heavier, more complex Lord of the Rings adaptations.
