## Design Notes

Designer: Jarratt Gray & Carl de Visser. Published by Burnt Island Games (2024).

### Theme Integration

Endeavor: Deep Sea reimagines the Endeavor franchise as an oceanic research
expedition, replacing the colonial exploration theme of the original Age of
Sail with something more contemporary and morally uncomplicated: the
scientific exploration of the deep ocean. Players lead marine research
organizations deploying submarines, building research stations, and
collecting biological specimens from increasingly deep ocean zones. The
theme is not merely a reskin -- it fundamentally reshapes the game's
spatial logic. Where Age of Sail had continents to discover and trade
routes to establish, Deep Sea has vertical depth zones that represent
progressively more extreme environments, each requiring more advanced
technology to access and yielding rarer specimens.

The depth-as-difficulty metaphor is elegantly expressed through the game's
mechanics. Shallow zones are accessible early and yield common specimens;
deep zones require upgraded submarines and specialized crew but contain
rare species worth more points. This vertical progression mirrors real
oceanography -- the sunlight zone teems with familiar life, the twilight
zone holds strange creatures, the midnight zone is hostile and alien, and
the abyssal zone is nearly unreachable. Players feel the increasing
pressure (literal and figurative) as they push deeper, investing
progressively more resources for progressively greater rewards. The
theme transforms what could be an abstract engine-building exercise
into a narrative of scientific ambition.

The specimen collection system reinforces the educational dimension. Each
specimen card depicts a real or plausible deep-sea organism, and collecting
sets of specimens from different taxonomic groups mirrors the work of real
marine biologists cataloging biodiversity. The research station network
represents institutional infrastructure -- you are not just exploring
alone but building a permanent scientific presence in the ocean. The
theme succeeds because it gives every mechanical action a narrative
context: deploying a submarine is sending an expedition, building a
station is establishing a research outpost, collecting a specimen is
making a scientific discovery.

### Core Loop

Each round follows a structured sequence of deployment and retrieval:

1. **BUILDING PHASE** -- Each player selects one new building from the
   available row to add to their player board. Buildings provide worker
   slots, special abilities, and end-game scoring conditions. The
   selection is constrained by your current tracks -- higher track
   positions unlock access to more powerful buildings.

2. **DEPLOYMENT PHASE** -- Players take turns placing workers from their
   available pool onto:
   a. Their own building slots (activating the building's ability)
   b. Shared ocean board spaces (deploying submarines to zones, claiming
      route connections, establishing presence at research stations)
   c. Special action spaces that provide one-time benefits

3. **ACTION RESOLUTION** -- When a worker is placed, its action resolves
   immediately. Actions include:
   - Deploy submarine to an ocean zone (claim a specimen or route token)
   - Build a research station (establish presence, unlock zone access)
   - Advance on a track (Science, Engineering, Navigation, or Influence)
   - Draw cards from the specimen deck
   - Trade specimens for victory points

4. **RETRIEVAL PHASE** -- Workers placed on buildings are retrieved and
   returned to the available pool. Workers placed on the ocean board
   remain deployed (they are committed to the field). Some buildings
   grant bonus effects during retrieval, creating an incentive to
   cycle workers through specific buildings repeatedly.

5. **UPKEEP** -- Advance the round tracker, refresh the building row,
   and check for end-game conditions. The game spans five rounds, with
   escalating building power and deeper ocean access as the campaign
   of exploration unfolds.

### Key Design Patterns

DEPTH ZONES AS PROGRESSIVE DIFFICULTY/REWARD TIERS: The ocean board is
organized into horizontal bands representing depth zones -- from the
sunlit shallows at the top to the crushing abyss at the bottom. Shallower
zones are cheaper to access (fewer prerequisites, lower action cost) but
yield less valuable specimens and connections. Deeper zones require
upgraded submarines, higher track positions, and more worker investment,
but they contain rare specimens, high-value connections, and powerful
bonuses. This tiered structure creates a natural progression arc within
each game: players begin in shallow waters building basic capabilities,
then gradually extend their reach deeper as their engine matures. The
design lesson is that progressive access gating -- where content becomes
available as players invest in prerequisites -- creates a satisfying
sense of growth without requiring explicit level-up mechanics.

ACTION RETRIEVAL CYCLE: The core rhythm of Endeavor: Deep Sea is deploy-
then-retrieve. Workers placed on your own buildings are retrieved each
round, creating a renewable action pool. Workers committed to the ocean
board, however, stay deployed -- they represent permanent field presence.
This creates a fundamental tension: building actions are renewable but
local, while ocean actions are powerful but consume workers permanently.
As the game progresses, your available worker pool shrinks unless you
invest in buildings that generate new workers or provide retrieval
bonuses. The retrieval cycle rewards planning across multiple rounds --
a building that seems modest on its first activation becomes powerful
when you realize you will retrieve and re-deploy that worker four more
times. The design pattern teaches that distinguishing renewable from
consumable actions creates strategic depth around resource allocation
over time.

BUILDING TRACKS AS ENGINE CONSTRUCTION: Four advancement tracks --
Science, Engineering, Navigation, and Influence -- govern access to
buildings, ocean zones, and special abilities. Each track position
unlocks new capabilities: higher Science grants access to advanced
buildings, higher Engineering allows deeper submarine deployment,
higher Navigation enables longer routes, and higher Influence provides
more workers and political power. The tracks are the game's engine:
advancing them is how you expand your action vocabulary and reach.
The design elegance is that track advancement is itself an action
requiring worker placement, creating a bootstrapping problem -- you
must invest actions in capability expansion before you can take
the actions that expansion enables.

SPECIMEN COLLECTION AS SET COLLECTION: Specimens collected from ocean
zones come in multiple taxonomic categories, and scoring rewards
diversity within and across categories. Collecting a complete set of
specimens from one taxonomic group yields bonus points; collecting
specimens from many different groups yields breadth bonuses. The set
collection creates a valuation puzzle at every specimen acquisition:
do you specialize deeply in one category for completion bonuses or
diversify broadly for coverage bonuses? This tension interacts with
the depth zone structure -- deeper zones contain rarer specimens that
are more valuable for set completion but harder to access.

RESEARCH STATION NETWORK AS AREA PRESENCE: Building research stations
on the ocean board establishes permanent presence that provides ongoing
benefits -- route connections, zone access, and adjacency bonuses.
Stations interact with the depth zone system because deeper stations
are harder to build but provide access to more valuable zones. The
network aspect means that station placement is not just about
individual location value but about connectivity -- a chain of
stations reaching from shallow to deep zones creates an infrastructure
pipeline that reduces the cost of deep exploration. The design pattern
is that permanent board presence creates compound value over time,
rewarding early investment in infrastructure.

CREW SPECIALIZATION THROUGH UPGRADE PATHS: Workers are not
interchangeable -- they can be specialized through upgrade paths
that grant them enhanced capabilities. A basic worker might only
access shallow zones, while an upgraded worker can operate in deep
zones or activate powerful building abilities. Specialization creates
divergent worker identities within your pool, meaning that worker
allocation becomes a matching problem: assign the right worker to
the right task. The design consequence is that your workforce develops
a character over the game, with specialists and generalists serving
different strategic functions.

SHARED OCEAN BOARD AS INTERACTION SPACE: While each player has their
own building tableau, the ocean board is shared and competitive. Route
connections, station locations, and specimen availability are contested
resources. When one player claims a route token or establishes a
station, that space is no longer available to others. The shared board
creates interaction without direct conflict -- you are not attacking
opponents but competing for limited positional advantage in the same
physical space. The tension between developing your private engine
(buildings and tracks) and competing on the shared board (ocean
presence) is the game's central strategic balance.

DEPTH PRESSURE AS RESOURCE COST ESCALATION: The deeper you go, the
more everything costs. Deep-zone submarines require more track
advancement, more specialized workers, and more actions to deploy.
This escalating cost structure mirrors real deep-sea operations, where
pressure, darkness, and distance compound the difficulty of every
activity. The design function of cost escalation is pacing: it prevents
players from rushing to the most valuable content and ensures that the
early game (shallow exploration) and late game (deep expeditions) feel
meaningfully different. The escalation also creates catch-up potential
-- a player who falls behind in shallow zones can invest in track
advancement and leapfrog to deep-zone opportunities that early
leaders have not yet accessed.

FIVE-ROUND ARC AS NARRATIVE STRUCTURE: The game's fixed five-round
structure creates a clear narrative arc: expedition planning (round 1),
initial exploration (round 2), expansion and specialization (round 3),
deep-sea push (round 4), and final scoring optimization (round 5). Each
round introduces more powerful buildings into the available pool, meaning
that the game's action vocabulary expands naturally over time. The
five-round constraint also creates urgency -- with only five cycles of
deployment and retrieval, every worker placement carries significant
opportunity cost. The design lesson is that fixed-length games with
escalating content create natural story arcs without requiring
explicit narrative mechanisms.

### What Makes It #95

Endeavor: Deep Sea succeeds by finding the intersection of engine building,
worker placement, and spatial exploration, then grounding that intersection
in a theme that makes every mechanism legible. The depth-zone structure
gives the game a vertical progression that feels qualitatively different
from the horizontal expansion of most exploration games -- you are not
spreading outward across a map but pushing downward into increasingly
hostile territory, and the mechanical cost escalation mirrors that
physical reality. The action retrieval cycle, inherited and refined from
the original Endeavor, creates a rhythm of investment and return that
rewards long-term planning over opportunistic play. Gray and de Visser
have built a game where the theme of scientific exploration is not
decorative but structural -- every system in the game is shaped by the
question of what it means to venture into the unknown, one depth zone
at a time.
