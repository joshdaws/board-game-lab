## Design Notes

Designers: S J Macdonald, Shem Phillips. Published by Garphill Games (2020).

### Theme Integration

Viscounts of the West Kingdom completes Garphill Games' West Kingdom trilogy
(following Architects and Paladins) by placing players as noble viscounts
navigating the political, economic, and spiritual landscape of Carolingian
Francia. Where Architects explored building and Paladins explored defense,
Viscounts focuses on influence — the currency of medieval nobility. Players
travel a circular path around the kingdom, representing the itinerant nature
of medieval governance. Unlike modern political figures rooted in a capital,
Carolingian nobles moved constantly through their territories, and the
rondel mechanism captures this peripatetic reality. Your viscount does not
sit in a throne room making decisions; they ride from town to castle to
monastery, building influence through physical presence and strategic
engagement.

The deck-building mechanism represents the viscount's growing retinue of
advisors, craftsmen, clerics, and merchants. Each card in your deck is a
person in your entourage — their abilities become available when drawn into
your hand and played during visits to locations around the rondel. Acquiring
new cards models recruiting specialists to your court, while the innovative
"transcribing" mechanism (removing cards to the manuscript track) represents
dedicating your followers to the great work of preserving knowledge in the
kingdom's scriptoriums. This dual-use of cards — play them for abilities or
transcribe them for manuscript bonuses — captures the medieval tension between
worldly power (keeping talented people in your service) and spiritual legacy
(contributing to the preservation of civilization).

The three influence tracks — the castle, the town, and the monastery —
represent the three pillars of medieval power: military authority, civic
prosperity, and religious devotion. Each track offers different rewards and
end-game scoring opportunities, and the area majority competition on each
track models the zero-sum nature of medieval influence. A viscount who invests
heavily in the church does so at the expense of military or commercial
development. The virtue-corruption axis, a signature of the West Kingdom
trilogy, adds moral dimension: certain actions increase your corruption
(representing pragmatic but ethically questionable choices), while others
maintain virtue. Neither extreme is inherently superior — corruption unlocks
powerful abilities while virtue provides scoring advantages — modeling the
genuine moral ambiguity of medieval governance.

### Core Loop

The game is played over a variable number of rounds until one of three
end-game triggers is met (castle, town, or manuscript track completion).
Players take turns in clockwise order.

**On Your Turn:**

1. MOVE YOUR VISCOUNT — Move your viscount 1-3 spaces clockwise around
   the rondel. Each space on the rondel represents a location with a
   specific action type. Your movement distance determines which locations
   are available to you this turn.
   a) Some spaces are enhanced by building tiles placed during the game,
      increasing the reward for stopping there
   b) Movement distance interacts with your played cards — certain cards
      grant additional movement or modify movement rules

2. PLAY CARDS — Play cards from your hand to your player board. Cards
   provide:
   a) Resources (gold, stone, ink) used for construction and transcription
   b) Worker types (clerics, merchants, craftsmen, criminals) that
      determine which actions are available and how powerful they are
   c) Special abilities that trigger during specific phases of your turn
   d) Card placement follows hand management principles — you choose
      which cards to play and which to hold for future turns

3. PERFORM LOCATION ACTION — Based on where your viscount stopped on
   the rondel, perform one of four primary actions:
   a) CASTLE — Place workers on the castle track, contributing to area
      majority scoring and advancing military influence. Workers placed
      depend on card types played this turn. Advance the castle building
      track for bonuses.
   b) TOWN — Engage in commerce, acquiring resources or trading goods.
      Merchant cards enhance town actions. Place workers for town-track
      area majority.
   c) MONASTERY — Transcribe cards from your hand or discard pile to the
      manuscript track. Transcribing removes a card from your deck
      permanently (deck thinning) and advances your manuscript position.
      Cleric cards enhance monastery actions.
   d) WORKSHOP — Build structures on the rondel, permanently enhancing
      specific locations for yourself and potentially for others. Costs
      resources (stone, gold).

4. RECRUIT OR DISMISS — Optionally acquire new cards from the available
   townsfolk display (adding to your deck) or dismiss cards from your
   hand to manage deck composition. Recruiting costs gold.
   a) The townsfolk display is an open draft from the shared supply
   b) Dismissed cards may go to the poverty track, affecting the
      virtue-corruption axis

5. DRAW BACK UP — Refill your hand from your personal deck. If your
   deck is empty, shuffle your discard pile to form a new deck.

**End-Game Triggers:** The game ends when any player reaches the top of
the castle track, the town track, or completes a full manuscript row.
Final scoring includes: area majority on castle, town, and monastery
tracks; manuscript bonuses; building bonuses; end-game bonus cards;
virtue/corruption scoring; and debt penalties.

### Key Design Patterns

RONDEL AS CONSTRAINED MOVEMENT: The circular rondel constrains player
movement to 1-3 spaces in one direction, creating an elegant tension
between desire and access. Unlike free movement systems where any
action is always available, the rondel means that reaching a specific
location requires planning several turns ahead. If you need the
monastery but just passed it, you must circuit most of the board to
return. This spatial constraint on action selection adds a planning
dimension absent from standard action selection menus. The rondel also
creates natural player interaction through location contention —
buildings placed on rondel spaces can be used by whoever stops there,
and timing your visits to avoid or exploit opponents' buildings becomes
a key strategic consideration.

DECK BUILDING MEETS DECK THINNING: Viscounts integrates two opposing
deck-manipulation forces — recruitment (adding cards) and transcription
(removing cards) — into a unified strategic decision. Standard deck
builders treat thinning as a secondary concern or ignore it entirely.
Here, transcription is a primary action tied to the monastery and the
manuscript track, meaning deck thinning is not just an efficiency
optimization but a scoring path in its own right. The tension is real:
every transcribed card improves your deck's consistency but permanently
removes an ability from your repertoire. Do you transcribe your weakest
cards for marginal deck improvement, or transcribe strong cards that
grant manuscript bonuses? The design principle is that deck manipulation
becomes strategic when both adding and removing cards carry genuine
opportunity costs.

MULTI-TRACK AREA MAJORITY: Three separate area majority competitions
(castle, town, monastery) run simultaneously, each advancing through
different actions. This structure forces diversification while rewarding
specialization in one or two tracks — you cannot ignore any track
entirely without ceding majority points to opponents, but spreading too
thin prevents achieving the bonuses at the top of any single track.
The three tracks interact with the rondel: castle actions are available
at castle locations, monastery actions at monastery locations, creating
spatial pressure that compounds the strategic challenge. The design
insight is that multiple competing majority tracks create richer
interaction than a single majority contest, because players must read
which tracks opponents are contesting and choose where to compete
versus concede.

MODULAR BOARD AS REPLAYABILITY: The rondel's building spaces accept
tiles placed during the game, creating a board state that evolves with
each session. Early buildings shape the strategic landscape for the
entire game — a cluster of enhanced monastery spaces creates a
transcription-favorable rondel, while enhanced castle spaces shift
the incentive toward military investment. Because building is
competitive (spaces are limited and buildings are permanent), early
game decisions about which locations to enhance create lasting
strategic consequences. The modular board ensures no two games present
identical rondel topography, preventing static opening strategies.

VIRTUE-CORRUPTION AS STRATEGIC SPECTRUM: The virtue-corruption axis
is not a morality system but a strategic dial. Virtuous players gain
access to scoring multipliers and certain card abilities; corrupt
players unlock powerful action enhancements and resource discounts.
Moving along the axis is not inherently good or bad — it depends
on your strategic trajectory. This design avoids the trap of making
"good" choices mechanically inferior (which punishes ethical play) or
mechanically superior (which makes the choice trivial). Instead, both
ends of the spectrum offer genuine advantages, and the optimal position
depends on your deck composition, track position, and opponents'
strategies. The generalizable principle is that moral axes in games
work best when both directions offer meaningful mechanical rewards.

MANUSCRIPT TRACK AS PARALLEL ECONOMY: The manuscript track creates an
entirely separate scoring economy from the area majority tracks.
Transcribing cards advances your position on a shared manuscript grid,
where specific rows and columns provide bonuses and VP. The manuscript
rewards long-term planning — transcribing specific card types to
complete rows is more valuable than random transcription. This parallel
economy ensures that deck thinning is never purely about efficiency;
it is about pursuing specific manuscript objectives that may conflict
with your deck's functional needs.

END-GAME TRIGGER RACING: Three independent end-game triggers (castle,
town, or manuscript completion) create a race dynamic where players
can force the game to end on their preferred timeline. A player
dominating the castle track can rush its completion to end the game
before monastery-focused opponents fully develop their manuscript
scoring. This trigger-racing mechanism adds meta-strategic depth:
you must monitor not only your own scoring trajectory but also
opponents' pace toward triggering the end. The threat of a premature
end discourages purely long-term strategies and rewards players who
balance immediate scoring against engine development.

### What Makes It #194

Viscounts of the West Kingdom stands as the most mechanically ambitious
entry in the West Kingdom trilogy and one of the most successful
rondel-deck-building hybrids in modern design. Phillips and Macdonald
demonstrate that the rondel — an older mechanism often associated with
economic simulation games — gains new life when combined with deck
building's adaptive engine construction. The result is a game where
movement, card play, area majority, and deck management interact in
ways that create genuine strategic depth at a manageable 60-90 minute
play time. Its ranking reflects a design that offers experienced gamers
a dense decision space without the rules overhead of heavier titles,
while the West Kingdom setting and virtue-corruption axis give every
mechanical choice a thematic resonance that pure abstractions lack.
