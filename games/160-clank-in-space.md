# Clank! In! Space!: A Deck-Building Adventure

## Design Notes

Designer: Paul Dennen. Published by Dire Wolf / Renegade Game Studios (2017).

### Theme Integration

Clank! In! Space! reimagines the original Clank!'s dungeon-delving heist
as a science fiction infiltration of Lord Eradikus's spaceship, a massive
vessel packed with treasure, security systems, and hostile denizens. The
thematic premise is immediately compelling: players are rival thieves who
have smuggled themselves aboard an evil overlord's flagship, and they must
steal artifacts, evade detection, and escape before the ship's security
forces overwhelm them. The "clank" in the title refers to the noise each
thief generates --- represented by cubes added to a shared bag --- which
accumulates until Lord Eradikus attacks and random cubes are drawn from the
bag, potentially damaging the noisiest thieves. The theme is perfectly
aligned with the press-your-luck core: the deeper you penetrate into the
ship, the better the treasure, but the more noise you generate and the
harder it becomes to escape alive.

The spaceship setting provides structural advantages over the original
game's dungeon. The modular board is divided into two connected wings with
specialized areas: cargo bays, data archives, command modules, and escape
pods. Movement through the ship requires navigating locked corridors that
demand specific card abilities (hacking, combat, movement points) to
traverse, and the spatial layout creates meaningful route-planning decisions
that the original game's relatively simple dungeon did not demand. The
science fiction setting also introduces faction-specific market cards ---
mercenaries, space smugglers, and alien tech dealers --- whose thematic
identities communicate their mechanical functions intuitively. A bounty
hunter card deals damage; a hacking module opens locked doors; a fusion
core provides energy (the game's currency). The theme does real work here,
making card evaluation faster and more intuitive than raw mechanical text
would allow.

Lord Eradikus himself is the game's most successful thematic mechanism. As
the antagonist, he represents the ticking clock that prevents players from
exploring indefinitely. His attacks escalate over the course of the game as
the "boss track" advances: early attacks draw few cubes from the bag (low
danger), while late attacks draw many (potentially lethal). When a player
is knocked out deep inside the ship, they are eliminated from scoring. When
a player escapes to an escape pod, they are safe but can no longer
improve their haul. The escalation creates a narrative arc --- early
confidence gives way to mounting dread --- that mirrors the heist genre's
traditional structure: the plan works smoothly until it doesn't, and then
everything happens at once.

### Core Loop

Players take turns in clockwise order. On each turn, a player draws five
cards from their personal deck, plays them to generate resources and
effects, then buys new cards and resolves board movement. The game ends
when all players have escaped or been knocked out.

**Turn Structure:**

1. PLAY CARDS --- Play any or all cards from your hand of five. Cards
   generate four types of resources:
   - SKILL (blue): Currency used to purchase new cards from the market row
   - SWORDS (red): Combat value used to defeat enemies on the board and
     in the market row
   - BOOTS (yellow): Movement points used to traverse corridors on the
     ship
   - CLANK: Noise generated; for each clank symbol, add one of your
     colored cubes to the clank area (which will later be added to the
     shared bag)
   Cards may also provide special abilities: drawing additional cards,
   trashing cards from your hand or discard, healing damage, or
   interacting with specific board elements.

2. ACQUIRE CARDS --- Spend skill points to purchase cards from the market
   row of six face-up cards. Purchased cards go to your discard pile (the
   standard deckbuilding delayed purchase). The market row is immediately
   refilled from the adventure deck. Some cards in the market are enemies
   rather than purchases: defeat them with swords to gain rewards (coins,
   healing, or other benefits). Defeating certain enemies also advances
   the boss track, bringing Lord Eradikus's next attack closer.

3. MOVE ON THE BOARD --- Spend boot points to move your pawn through the
   ship's corridors. Each corridor has a movement cost (typically one or
   two boots) and may have additional requirements:
   - LOCKED CORRIDORS require spending a specific resource (hack symbols,
     swords) to pass through
   - ONE-WAY CORRIDORS can only be traversed in the indicated direction
   - CRYSTAL CORRIDORS require spending a crystal resource
   Movement allows you to reach rooms containing artifacts (high-value
   treasures), minor loot tokens, data archives, and other rewards.

4. COLLECT LOOT --- When entering a room with available loot, take it.
   Artifacts are the primary victory point source and can only be found
   deep inside the ship. Minor loot tokens provide small bonuses (credits,
   healing, bonus movement). Data archives provide end-game scoring
   opportunities based on set collection.

5. GENERATE CLANK --- After all cards are played, add your accumulated
   clank cubes to the clank area on the board. These cubes will be
   deposited into the shared bag during the next dragon attack.

6. BOSS ATTACK --- When the boss track advances to an attack symbol (or
   when certain market cards trigger an attack), resolve Lord Eradikus's
   assault:
   a) Move all cubes from the clank area into the shared bag
   b) Draw a number of cubes from the bag equal to the current attack
      value. Black cubes (neutral) are harmless; colored cubes deal one
      damage to the corresponding player.
   c) Each player has a health track. When all health is depleted, the
      player is knocked out. If knocked out inside the ship (not in an
      escape pod), the player scores nothing. If knocked out in the escape
      pod area, the player scores but at a penalty.

7. CLEANUP --- Discard all played and unplayed cards from hand. Draw five
   new cards from your deck (shuffle discard into deck when draw pile is
   empty).

**Endgame Triggers:**

When a player reaches an escape pod and escapes the ship, the boss track
begins advancing automatically each round, escalating attacks against
remaining players. This creates urgency: once one player escapes, all
remaining players face increasing danger. The game ends when all players
have escaped or been knocked out. Players score artifacts, collected
loot, coins, and data archives, minus any penalties. Highest score wins;
eliminated players (knocked out inside the ship, not near escape pods)
score zero.

### Key Design Patterns

PRESS YOUR LUCK AS STRUCTURAL NARRATIVE: The game's defining tension is
the push-pull between penetrating deeper into the ship for better treasure
and retreating to safety before Lord Eradikus's attacks become lethal.
This press-your-luck dynamic generates a natural narrative arc for every
game: cautious exploration, followed by the critical decision of when to
turn back, followed by a frantic escape race. The design genius is that
this arc emerges organically from the spatial layout and escalation
mechanics rather than being scripted. Players who push too deep risk
elimination; players who retreat too early leave high-value artifacts on
the table. The right moment to turn back is never obvious, and the
uncertainty is what makes the decision compelling.

CLANK AS PROBABILISTIC RISK: The clank-and-bag mechanism is an elegant
probabilistic system. Your noise (colored cubes) enters a shared bag
alongside other players' cubes and neutral black cubes. When an attack
occurs, cubes are drawn randomly. A player who has generated more clank
has more cubes in the bag and is therefore statistically more likely to
take damage. But the randomness means that a noisy player might escape
unscathed while a quiet player draws unlucky. This probabilistic risk
is more interesting than deterministic damage because it creates hope ---
you might get lucky --- while still punishing noise over time. The shared
bag also creates indirect interaction: adding your cubes dilutes other
players' risk, and vice versa.

DECKBUILDING MEETS BOARD MOVEMENT: Clank! In! Space! was among the first
designs to integrate deckbuilding with physical board movement, and the
combination produces strategic depth that neither mechanism generates
alone. Your deck determines not just what you can buy (as in pure
deckbuilders) but where you can go and how fast you can get there. A deck
heavy on boots enables rapid traversal but may lack the swords to defeat
guardians or the hacking symbols to open locked corridors. Building your
deck is simultaneously building your movement capabilities, your combat
abilities, and your noise profile. This integration means that market
purchases are evaluated not just on their card-level efficiency but on
their contribution to your spatial strategy.

MODULAR BOARD AS ROUTE PUZZLE: The ship's layout --- two wings connected
by a central corridor, with rooms arranged in a branching network of
locked and unlocked paths --- creates a route-planning puzzle that layers
over the deckbuilding. Players must plot a path through the ship that
maximizes treasure collection while minimizing the number of turns spent
deep inside (where danger accumulates). Different board configurations
(the game includes double-sided modules) create different optimal routes,
and the market's random card offerings mean that the resources available
for navigation vary from game to game. Route planning and deck
construction inform each other: you build your deck to navigate your
chosen route, and you choose your route based on the cards available.

DELAYED PURCHASE AS TIMING CONSTRAINT: Like all deckbuilders, purchased
cards go to your discard pile and only become available after a shuffle.
In Clank! In! Space!, this delay has spatial consequences: a hacking
module purchased on turn three will not appear in your hand for several
turns, meaning the locked corridor it would open is inaccessible until
then. Players must anticipate their deck's shuffle timing when planning
their route through the ship. Purchasing a movement card just before a
shuffle cycles it into availability quickly; purchasing it just after
means a long wait. This temporal planning layer distinguishes the game
from deckbuilders where shuffle timing is less consequentially connected
to board state.

ESCAPE AS COMPETITIVE TRIGGER: When the first player escapes the ship,
the boss track begins escalating automatically, creating mounting pressure
on remaining players. This mechanism transforms a cooperative-adjacent
tension (everyone is threatened by the same boss) into a competitive one
(the first player to escape actively endangers the others). A player who
escapes early may sacrifice potential treasure but guarantees their score
and imposes a ticking clock on opponents. This competitive trigger prevents
the "parallel solitaire" problem common to deckbuilders: your decisions
about when to flee directly affect other players' survival.

PLAYER ELIMINATION AS STAKES AMPLIFIER: The possibility of scoring zero ---
being knocked out inside the ship with no points at all --- raises the
stakes of every decision in the game's second half. In most Euro-style
games, poor decisions lead to low scores; in Clank! In! Space!, poor
decisions can lead to no score. This amplified consequence makes the
press-your-luck decision genuinely tense in a way that mere point
optimization cannot achieve. The risk of total loss transforms what
could be a casual deckbuilding exercise into a game where the final
rounds provoke genuine anxiety.

MARKET ROW AS SHARED OPPORTUNITY SPACE: The six-card market row, refilled
from a shared adventure deck, creates an open-drafting dynamic where
players compete for the most synergistic cards. A powerful hacking card
is worth more to the player planning a route through locked corridors
than to one taking an open path. The market row also contains enemies that
must be defeated with swords; choosing when to fight market enemies (for
their rewards) versus when to buy cards (for long-term deck improvement)
is a recurring tactical decision. The shared market ensures that players
are never operating in isolation --- every purchase and every fight changes
the options available to opponents.

### What Makes It #160

Clank! In! Space! refined the hybrid deckbuilder-board-game formula that
its predecessor introduced, and the science fiction reskin proved to be
more than cosmetic. The modular board, locked corridors, and data archive
set collection add spatial and strategic depth that the original's simpler
dungeon lacked. The game occupies a rare design niche: it is accessible
enough for deckbuilding newcomers (the core loop is buy-cards-play-cards)
while providing enough strategic texture (route planning, timing, risk
assessment) to satisfy experienced players. The press-your-luck arc ---
that moment when you must decide whether to grab one more artifact or
run for the escape pods --- generates memorable table moments that pure
optimization games seldom produce. At 2.58 weight, it delivers tension,
narrative, and strategic depth in a package that consistently earns its
place among the most highly regarded medium-weight designs.
