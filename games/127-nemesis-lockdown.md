## Design Notes

Designer: Adam Kwapiński. Published by Awaken Realms (2022).

### Theme Integration

Nemesis: Lockdown transposes the original Nemesis formula from a
drifting spaceship to a Mars research base, and this setting change
is not cosmetic — it fundamentally reshapes how the horror operates.
A spaceship is a sealed tube where every corridor leads to more
corridors; a planetary base has an inside and an outside, rooms that
can be sealed or vented, and an environment beyond the walls that is
itself hostile. The facility's lockdown systems — blast doors, power
grids, quarantine protocols — become both survival tools and
potential death traps. Sealing a section might protect you from
Nightstalkers, but it might also trap a fellow player inside with
them. The Mars surface is not safety; it is a different kind of
danger, trading alien predators for environmental exposure.

The Nightstalkers replace the original game's Intruders as the alien
threat, and their behavioral differences reinforce the new setting's
identity. Where Intruders were aggressive biological swarm organisms
suited to the claustrophobia of a spaceship, Nightstalkers are
adapted to the Martian environment — they interact with the base's
power systems, exploit darkness, and behave more tactically. The
encounter deck that drives alien behavior creates emergent narrative:
you do not script horror, you generate it procedurally through card
draws and die rolls, ensuring that every session produces a unique
survival story. The noise system — where actions generate noise
tokens that attract aliens — models the tension of trying to move
quietly through a compromised facility, and the escalation from
noise to encounter to combat to potential death follows the classic
horror arc of dread building toward confrontation.

The semi-cooperative structure is where theme and mechanism most
powerfully converge. Each player has a secret personal objective that
may require survival, research completion, specimen collection, or
even the death of other players. The horror genre's trope of
competing agendas under existential threat — think Alien's Ash
or The Thing's paranoid distrust — is mechanically encoded. You
need cooperation to survive the Nightstalkers, but you cannot fully
trust anyone because their objective might be served by your failure.
This tension between collective survival and individual ambition is
the game's emotional engine, and it cannot be separated from the
horror theme without collapsing the design.

### Core Loop

The game plays over 15 rounds maximum, though it frequently ends
earlier through player death, escape, or objective completion. Each
round has two phases:

**PLAYER PHASE**

1. Each player has 2 action tokens. On your turn, spend one token to
   perform one action. Play proceeds clockwise until all players have
   spent both tokens or passed.
2. Available actions include:
   a. MOVE — Move to an adjacent room. If the room is unexplored,
      draw a room tile and place it, potentially triggering
      exploration events. Moving generates noise in both the room
      you leave and the room you enter.
   b. SEARCH — Draw item cards from the room's search deck. Items
      include weapons, tools, medical supplies, and key cards for
      facility systems.
   c. INTERACT — Use a room's specific function: computer terminals
      for research, medical bays for healing, control rooms for
      facility management, airlocks for escape preparation.
   d. SHOOT — Attack an alien in your room using a weapon card.
      Combat uses dice, with weapon type determining the dice pool
      and damage thresholds.
   e. MELEE — Desperate close combat without a ranged weapon. High
      risk, last resort.
   f. FACILITY ACTIONS — Activate or deactivate power in sections,
      seal or unseal doors, trigger lockdown protocols. These
      actions manipulate the base's infrastructure.
   g. TRADE/GIVE — Exchange items with players in the same room.
   h. CRAFT — Combine specific items into more powerful equipment.

**EVENT PHASE**

3. Draw an event card that advances the narrative: alien movement,
   facility malfunctions, environmental hazards, or escalation of
   the Nightstalker threat. Noise tokens are resolved — rooms with
   sufficient noise may spawn alien encounters.
4. The time track advances. If it reaches the final space, the game
   ends immediately.

**End Conditions:** The game ends when all players have either escaped
the facility, died, or the time track expires. Each surviving player
checks their secret objective — you can survive but still lose if
your personal goal was not achieved.

### Key Design Patterns

SEMI-COOPERATIVE TRUST ARCHITECTURE: The game's most sophisticated
design element is its trust system. Players share a survival problem
that genuinely requires cooperation — lone players die quickly — but
each player's secret objective introduces private incentives that may
diverge from the group's interest. The design never forces betrayal;
it merely creates conditions where betrayal becomes rational for
some objectives. This ambiguity is more powerful than a binary traitor
mechanism because every player is potentially trustworthy and
potentially dangerous, and the uncertainty persists throughout the
entire game rather than resolving in a single reveal moment.

NOISE AS TENSION CURRENCY: Nearly every action generates noise tokens
in your current room or adjacent rooms. Noise accumulates until it
triggers an alien encounter, at which point the tokens are removed
and replaced with an alien miniature. This system transforms the
abstract concept of risk into a visible, escalating resource on the
board. Players can see exactly how much danger they are generating
and where, creating agonizing decisions about whether to search one
more time (adding noise) or leave before the threshold is reached.
The noise system also creates externalities — your noise can trigger
an encounter for another player in an adjacent room, making every
action a potential threat to your allies.

FACILITY SYSTEMS AS INTERACTIVE TERRAIN: The Mars base is not a
static map but a dynamic environment that players manipulate through
facility actions. Power can be rerouted, doors sealed, sections
locked down or vented. This transforms the board from a passive space
you move through into an active tool you wield. Sealing a door
behind you after fleeing an alien creates safety — but also blocks
other players from using that corridor. Venting a section kills
aliens but also any humans caught inside. The facility systems create
a second layer of strategic interaction beyond the alien threat:
players shape the environment itself, and those changes affect
everyone.

PROCEDURAL HORROR THROUGH ENCOUNTER DECKS: Rather than scripting
specific horror scenarios, the game generates narrative through
randomized encounter decks, room tile draws, and event cards. This
procedural approach means every session produces a unique story —
sometimes the aliens mass in one section while another stays eerily
quiet, sometimes a critical item appears early and shifts the entire
group's strategy, sometimes an event card triggers a catastrophic
failure at the worst possible moment. The procedural system captures
horror's essential unpredictability better than any scripted scenario
could, because genuine surprise requires genuine randomness.

PLAYER ELIMINATION WITH PURPOSE: Unlike many modern designs that avoid
player elimination, Nemesis: Lockdown embraces it as a core horror
mechanism. Characters can die — permanently — from alien attacks,
environmental hazards, or facility malfunctions. The threat of
elimination raises the stakes of every decision and makes the horror
feel consequential rather than abstract. The design mitigates the
negative experience of early elimination through the game's relatively
short effective playtime for eliminated players (the game accelerates
as the player count drops) and through the secret objective system,
which means a dead player may still achieve a posthumous victory if
their objective was fulfilled before death.

ASYMMETRIC CHARACTER CAPABILITIES: Each character has unique abilities,
starting equipment, and wound thresholds that create distinct survival
profiles. The Soldier excels at combat but lacks technical skills; the
Scientist can research efficiently but is fragile; the Engineer
manipulates facility systems. These asymmetries mean the group's
composition shapes its collective strategy — a group without a combat
specialist must avoid confrontation, while a group without a
researcher struggles to complete research objectives. The asymmetry
also feeds the trust problem: you need your teammates' unique
capabilities, but you cannot be certain those capabilities will be
used for the group's benefit.

### What Makes It #127

Nemesis: Lockdown succeeds because it commits fully to its genre
rather than hedging. The semi-cooperative trust problem, the noise
escalation system, the player elimination stakes, and the procedural
narrative generation all serve a single purpose: making you feel like
you are inside a survival horror film where the real danger might be
the person standing next to you. The Mars base setting differentiates
it from its predecessor through facility manipulation mechanics that
add strategic depth without diluting the horror. It sits at 3.90
weight because the rules overhead is genuine — this is not a casual
experience — but the thematic immersion justifies every rule. The
game's emotional payoff comes not from optimization but from the
stories it generates: the desperate last-round escape, the betrayal
that no one saw coming, the alien ambush in a room you thought was
safe. That narrative capacity, combined with mechanical substance,
is what earns its place on this list.
