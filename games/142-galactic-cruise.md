## Design Notes

### Theme Integration

Galactic Cruise immerses players in the fantasy of building and operating an
interstellar cruise line --- a premise that fuses the operational logistics of
terrestrial hospitality management with the expansive geography of space opera.
Players are not simply moving cubes through an economic engine; they are
charting routes between star systems, outfitting luxury vessels with amenities
that cater to alien passenger species, hiring specialized crew members, and
competing for the most lucrative travel corridors in the galaxy. The science
fiction setting is not window dressing. It provides the structural justification
for the game's network-building, crew specialization, and multi-species
passenger demand systems. A cruise line set in the Caribbean would not
naturally accommodate the mechanical diversity that alien species, exotic star
systems, and modular ship construction enable.

The passenger demand market is the game's thematic centerpiece. Different
alien species desire different amenities, destinations, and service levels,
creating a demand landscape that shifts across the game as species cards enter
and leave the market. Building a luxury spa module on your ship means nothing
if no passengers currently desire spa experiences; constructing a navigation
array matters only if your routes connect to destinations those passengers want
to visit. This demand-driven economy grounds the game's optimization puzzle in
a thematic logic that players can reason about intuitively: you are a cruise
operator trying to match your supply of experiences to the demand of a diverse
clientele, navigating a market that evolves as passenger preferences shift.

The multi-use card system gives Galactic Cruise its distinctive hand management
texture. Each card can serve as crew (placed on your ship for ongoing
abilities), as a ship upgrade (installed to add amenities or improve
performance), or as a resource spent to fuel other actions. This triple-use
design means that every card drawn presents a three-way evaluation puzzle
shaped by your current strategic position. A card that would be an excellent
crew member might also be the upgrade your ship desperately needs, and
discarding it for resources might fund the construction that makes your next
turn explosive. The hand is never comfortable --- there is always more value
in it than you can extract.

### Core Loop

The game unfolds over a variable number of rounds determined by a shared event
track. Each round consists of worker placement followed by action resolution:

1. CREW DEPLOYMENT --- Each player deploys their crew workers to action spaces
   on the central board and their personal ship board. Crew workers come in
   different specialization types (Engineering, Hospitality, Navigation,
   Commerce), and certain action spaces require specific crew types or provide
   enhanced benefits when staffed by matching specialists. The different worker
   types transform standard worker placement into a matching puzzle: you need
   the right specialist at the right station, and acquiring new specialists
   through hiring is itself an action that competes with other priorities.

2. ACTION RESOLUTION --- Players resolve their placed workers in a defined
   sequence across multiple action categories:
   a) DRAFT --- Select cards from the open display, adding them to hand.
      Cards in the display rotate each round, creating timing pressure.
   b) BUILD --- Install amenity modules and ship upgrades by paying resource
      costs. Modules are placed on your modular ship board, where adjacency
      bonuses reward spatial planning.
   c) HIRE --- Play crew cards from hand onto your ship board, gaining their
      ongoing abilities and adding to your worker pool for future rounds.
   d) ROUTE --- Extend your cruise network by connecting to new star systems
      on the modular galaxy board. Connections cost resources and may require
      Navigation crew.
   e) EMBARK --- Load passengers from the market onto your ship, matching
      their demand requirements to your available amenities, routes, and
      service capacity. Successfully embarked passengers generate income
      and victory points.
   f) TRADE --- Buy and sell goods at market prices, converting between
      resource types or generating cash.

3. INCOME AND UPKEEP --- Players collect income based on their passenger
   capacity, active crew bonuses, and route network extent. Upkeep costs
   for crew salaries and ship maintenance are deducted. Net income
   determines economic momentum into the next round.

4. MARKET REFRESH --- The passenger demand market shifts as new species
   cards are drawn and old demands expire. The open card display refreshes.
   The event track advances, potentially triggering end-game conditions
   or mid-game scoring events.

5. END GAME --- When the event track reaches its final position, a final
   scoring round occurs. End game bonuses reward set collection (diverse
   passenger species served), network completeness (star systems connected),
   and ship quality (amenity combinations installed).

### Key Design Patterns

MULTI-USE CARDS AS RESOURCE COMPRESSION: Every card in the game serves triple
duty as potential crew, ship upgrade, or expendable resource. This compression
means the deck is the game's entire economy in card form --- drawing cards is
not merely hand refilling but economic expansion, and every discard is a
strategic sacrifice. The multi-use design prevents the common Euro problem of
resource abundance in late game: because cards are always competing for
multiple uses, players never feel flush. There is always more demand for card
value than supply, maintaining tension throughout the arc.

WORKER PLACEMENT WITH CREW SPECIALIZATION: The different worker types
(Engineering, Hospitality, Navigation, Commerce) transform worker placement
from a pure blocking game into a workforce management puzzle. You cannot
simply place any worker at any space; optimal play requires matching
specialists to their corresponding actions. This creates a recruitment
metagame layered on top of the placement game: hiring decisions made in early
rounds constrain which actions you can efficiently execute in later rounds.
A player who invests heavily in Navigation crew can dominate route-building
but may lack the Hospitality specialists needed to embark high-value
passengers.

DEMAND-DRIVEN MARKET AS STRATEGIC COMPASS: The passenger demand market
provides direction to what could otherwise be an overwhelming optimization
space. Rather than calculating abstract point values for every possible action,
players can read the market to determine which amenities, routes, and crew
investments will actually convert into embarked passengers and victory points.
The shifting demand also prevents dominant strategies --- a player who builds
for one passenger species may find demand shifting toward another, requiring
adaptation. The market is both a strategic compass and a source of disruption.

MODULAR SHIP BOARD AS SPATIAL ENGINE: Ship upgrades and amenity modules are
placed on a personal ship board where adjacency matters. Placing a spa module
adjacent to a luxury cabin might unlock a combo bonus, while engineering bays
gain efficiency when clustered. This spatial dimension adds a layer of
planning beyond simple resource optimization: you are not just choosing what
to build but where to place it, and early placement decisions constrain
later options. The modular board also provides visual satisfaction --- watching
your ship grow from a bare hull to a fully equipped cruise liner creates a
tangible sense of progress.

NETWORK BUILDING AS ROUTE ECONOMICS: Extending your cruise line to new star
systems costs resources but unlocks access to destinations that specific
passenger species demand. Routes are not merely point-scoring connections;
they are economic infrastructure that enables passenger embarkation. A route
to a popular destination is worthless without the amenities passengers want,
and the best amenities are worthless without routes to where passengers want
to go. This bidirectional dependency between network and ship quality creates
a chicken-and-egg puzzle that different players resolve in different order,
producing strategic variety.

INCOME CYCLE AS ECONOMIC MOMENTUM: The income-and-upkeep cycle at each
round's end creates positive feedback for well-run cruise lines and
punishes overextension. Hired crew cost salaries, ship modules require
maintenance, and routes have upkeep costs. A player who expands too quickly
may find their income consumed by upkeep, while a player who builds
efficiently generates surplus that funds further expansion. The income
cycle is the game's pacing mechanism: it rewards measured growth over
explosive expansion.

VICTORY POINTS AS RESOURCE: Victory points earned during the game can be
spent on certain premium actions, creating an agonizing tradeoff between
scoring and investing. Spending VP to acquire a critical upgrade may generate
more VP in the long run, but the risk of miscalculation means you might end
the game having spent points you could not recoup. This mechanism prevents
comfortable point hoarding and forces players to remain engaged with the
economic engine even as end-game approaches.

### What Makes It #142

Galactic Cruise represents the 2025 wave of complex economic euros that push
weight boundaries while maintaining thematic coherence. At 3.98 weight, it
demands significant investment from players, but the cruise line theme provides
intuitive scaffolding for its interlocking systems: you are building ships,
hiring crew, charting routes, and attracting passengers, and every mechanism
maps to that operational fantasy. The multi-use card system ensures that
decisions are always constrained and meaningful, the crew specialization
adds texture to worker placement, and the demand-driven market prevents the
optimization paralysis that plagues many heavy euros. Its rapid ascent in the
rankings reflects both the contemporary appetite for complex economic games
and the design team's success in making weight serve theme rather than
existing for its own sake.
