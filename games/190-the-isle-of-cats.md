## Design Notes

Designer: Frank West. Published by The City of Games (2019).

### Theme Integration

The Isle of Cats tells the story of citizens rescuing cats from a
mysterious island before the villainous Lord Vesh arrives to destroy it.
Players load their boats with as many cats as possible, arranging the
polyomino-shaped cat tiles onto a personal ship grid to maximize both
the number of cats saved and the aesthetic arrangement of their rescue
vessel. The theme resonates with remarkable effectiveness despite its
whimsy — the urgency of the five-day countdown (five rounds) creates
genuine time pressure, and the spatial puzzle of fitting irregularly
shaped cats onto a boat grid evokes the physical challenge of
accommodating living creatures in a confined space. The color families
of cats (blue, green, red, purple, orange) represent different breeds,
and the set collection scoring for grouping same-colored cats together
models the comfort cats take from being near their own kind.

The card drafting mechanism is framed as the crew's preparation for each
day's rescue mission. Players draft discovery cards that determine which
rescue actions they can take, which special lessons (scoring conditions)
they pursue, and which treasures they find along the way. The drafting
represents the strategic decisions made before each day's expedition:
which routes to explore, which cat populations to prioritize, and what
provisions to bring. Lesson cards — secret objectives that score bonus
points for specific arrangements or collections — represent the wisdom
gained through the rescue process: learning that certain color groupings
are more harmonious, or that covering specific rooms on the ship
improves the cats' welfare. The thematic framework transforms what is
mechanically a polyomino puzzle with drafting into a narrative of
compassionate urgency.

The boat grid itself tells a story. Each player's ship has seven rooms,
and leaving a room completely empty at game end costs negative points —
representing the wasted space that could have sheltered more cats. The
rooms also contain printed treasure maps and rare treasures that score
bonus points when covered by cat tiles, rewarding players who plan
their placements to cover specific spaces. The ship is not a blank
canvas; it is a structured environment with its own demands, and the
spatial puzzle is enriched by the need to satisfy both the grid's
built-in objectives and the player's drafted lesson cards. The layered
spatial demands — cover rooms, group colors, reach treasures, fit shapes
— create a puzzle that is accessible on the surface but deeply strategic
in optimization.

### Core Loop

The game plays over exactly 5 rounds (days). Each round follows four
phases, with all players acting simultaneously during drafting and
sequentially during cat rescue.

**Phase 1 — FISHING (Income)**

1. Each player collects 20 fish from the supply. Fish are the game's
   currency, used to rescue cats and buy treasures.
2. Any fish unspent from previous rounds are retained.

**Phase 2 — EXPLORE (Card Drafting)**

1. Deal 7 discovery cards to each player from the shared deck.
2. DRAFT — Simultaneously, each player selects 2 cards from their
   hand and passes the remaining 5 to the left (rounds 1, 3, 5) or
   right (rounds 2, 4).
3. From the received hand of 5, select 2 more cards and pass the
   remaining 3. Continue until all cards are drafted.
4. Each player now holds 7 drafted cards. Cards come in several types:
   a) RESCUE CARDS — Required to rescue cats. Each shows a speed value
      (determines rescue order) and a cat color or type.
   b) LESSON CARDS — Secret objectives worth bonus VP at game end.
      Each costs fish to play (paid now, kept secret). Common lessons
      score for color groupings; rare lessons score for spatial
      patterns or specific arrangements.
   c) TREASURE CARDS — Provide treasures (small tiles) that can be
      placed on the ship grid for points.
   d) OSHAX CARDS — Wild cats that can be any color; powerful for
      completing color families.
   e) ANYTIME CARDS — Special ability cards playable at various
      points during the round.

5. Players simultaneously choose which lesson cards to play (paying
   fish costs) and which cards to keep for later phases. Lesson cards
   are placed face-down — their scoring conditions remain secret until
   end game.

**Phase 3 — READ THE FIELDS (Cat Display)**

1. Draw cat tiles from the bag and place them on the central fields
   display. The number drawn depends on player count.
2. Cat tiles are polyomino shapes (various sizes from 1-5 squares)
   in five colors. Each shape is unique.

**Phase 4 — RESCUE CATS (Sequential by Speed)**

1. Players take turns in order of their played rescue cards' speed
   values (fastest first).
2. On your turn, play one rescue card from your hand to rescue one
   cat tile from the display:
   a) The rescue card's color must match the cat tile's color (or be
      a wild/Oshax card).
   b) Pay fish equal to the cat tile's cost (based on position in
      the display — further cats cost more).
   c) Take the cat tile and immediately place it on your ship grid.
      The tile must be placed adjacent to an existing tile or touching
      the edge of the grid. Once placed, tiles cannot be moved.
3. Continue taking turns until all players have played all rescue
   cards they wish to use or cannot rescue any more cats.
4. Treasure tiles from treasure cards are also placed on the ship
   grid during this phase.

**SCORING (After Round 5)**

1. FAMILIES — Score for connected groups of same-colored cats:
   3 points for 3 cats, 5 for 4, 8 for 5, 11 for 6, and so on.
   Each connected group of the same color scores separately.
2. LESSONS — Reveal and score all secret lesson cards. Each lesson
   has specific conditions (e.g., "3 VP per cat in your largest
   color family," "5 VP if you have cats in all five colors,"
   "2 VP per room fully covered").
3. RARE TREASURES — Score 3 VP per rare treasure icon covered on
   the ship grid.
4. ROOMS — Lose 5 VP for each of the seven rooms on your ship that
   has zero cat tiles in it.
5. Total all points. Highest score wins.

### Key Design Patterns

POLYOMINO SPATIAL PUZZLE WITH CONSTRAINED PLACEMENT: Cat tiles are
polyomino shapes that must be placed adjacent to existing tiles or
the ship edge, and cannot be moved once placed. This constrained
placement rule transforms the spatial puzzle from a relaxed
optimization into a commitment game. Early placements shape the
available space for later tiles, and a poorly placed large cat in
round 1 can create dead zones that haunt you through round 5. The
constraint rewards forward planning — experienced players
mentally reserve areas for specific tile shapes and color families,
building their boat layout in their mind before committing tiles to
the grid. The generalizable principle is that irrevocable spatial
commitments create meaningful tension in tile placement games.

COLOR FAMILY SCORING AS SET COLLECTION: Same-colored cats in
connected groups score escalating points (3/5/8/11 for groups of
3/4/5/6). This scoring system creates a clear strategic incentive
to cluster same-colored cats together on the ship grid, which
often conflicts with the spatial efficiency of placing tiles
wherever they best fit. A red cat tile might perfectly fill a gap
on the left side of your ship, but your red family is growing on
the right side. The tension between spatial efficiency and color
grouping is the game's central decision axis. The escalating point
values (rather than linear) reward commitment to larger families,
creating a strategic push toward specialization that competes with
the diversification incentive of covering rooms and treasures.

CARD DRAFTING AS STRATEGIC PREPARATION: The drafting phase (7 cards,
pick 2, pass, repeat) is where the game's strategic decisions
concentrate. Players must simultaneously evaluate rescue cards
(which cats can I save?), lesson cards (which scoring objectives
suit my emerging ship layout?), and utility cards (which special
abilities do I need this round?). Because cards are drafted before
the cat tiles are revealed, drafting involves probabilistic
reasoning about which colors and shapes will appear. The closed
drafting format also creates hate-drafting opportunities —
denying a color-specific rescue card to an opponent building that
color family. Drafting is the mechanism that elevates Isle of Cats
above a simple spatial puzzle by adding strategic planning and
opponent awareness.

HIDDEN VICTORY POINTS VIA LESSON CARDS: Lesson cards are played
face-down and scored secretly at game end. This hidden information
creates uncertainty about relative standings throughout the game —
a player whose visible ship layout looks modest might have lesson
cards that dramatically increase their score. The hidden scoring
mechanism prevents the kingmaking problem that plagues many family
games (where trailing players can see exactly who is winning and
target them) and maintains suspense through the final reveal. It
also creates a private strategic dimension: each player has unique
scoring incentives visible only to them, meaning identical board
positions may be valued very differently by different players.

ROOM PENALTY AS COVERAGE INCENTIVE: Leaving any of the seven ship
rooms completely empty costs 5 VP — a significant penalty that
incentivizes broad coverage over concentrated placement. The room
penalty ensures that players cannot simply cluster all cats in one
area of the ship; they must spread placements to touch every room.
This creates a baseline spatial requirement that competes with
the color family incentive (which rewards clustering). The tension
between coverage (spread out to fill rooms) and grouping (cluster
to build families) is a core strategic axis that makes every tile
placement a genuine decision rather than an obvious optimization.

FISH ECONOMY AS RESOURCE MANAGEMENT: The 20-fish income per round
creates a budget constraint on cat rescue. Cats positioned further
from the island cost more fish, and lesson cards also cost fish to
play. Players must balance spending on cats (more tiles, better
spatial coverage) against spending on lessons (higher potential
end-game bonuses) against saving for future rounds (when more
expensive or desirable cats might appear). The fixed income
prevents runaway economic advantages and ensures all players have
roughly equal purchasing power each round, making the game's
competitive axis about spending efficiency rather than income
generation.

SPEED-BASED TURN ORDER IN RESCUE: Rescue cards have speed values
that determine the order in which players select cats from the
display. Faster cards give first pick — critical when a specific
color or shape is contested — but may offer other disadvantages.
The speed system creates a micro-auction dynamic during rescue:
multiple players wanting the same cat must have drafted faster
rescue cards to claim priority. This drafting-into-turn-order
pipeline connects the earlier strategic phase (drafting) to the
later tactical phase (rescue) in a satisfying causal chain.

### What Makes It #190

The Isle of Cats succeeds by layering genuine strategic depth beneath
one of the hobby's most inviting presentations. The cat theme and
colorful polyomino tiles attract players who might never try a
traditional Euro game, while the drafting mechanism, hidden scoring,
and multi-axis spatial puzzle reward repeated play and strategic
development. Frank West's key design insight was that the polyomino
genre, established by Patchwork and refined by A Feast for Odin,
could support a richer decision space through card drafting and set
collection without exceeding a 2.36 weight — the game adds strategic
layers through card synergies rather than rules complexity. The result
is a game that serves equally well as a family-weight introduction to
modern board gaming and as a satisfying puzzle for experienced players,
a dual audience that very few designs successfully capture.
