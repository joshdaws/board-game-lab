## Design Notes

Designer: Vladimír Suchý. Published by Delicious Games (2018).

### Theme Integration

Underwater Cities is set in a near-future world where overpopulation has forced
humanity beneath the ocean surface. Players represent visionary leaders tasked
with establishing self-sustaining underwater civilizations — networks of domed
cities connected by transportation tunnels, sustained by kelp farms,
desalination plants, and research laboratories. The theme is not merely
decorative science fiction; it maps directly onto the game's mechanical
structure. Cities must be physically connected via tunnels to function.
Buildings must be adjacent to connected cities to produce anything. Kelp farms
feed your growing population during production phases — fail to grow enough
kelp and your cities starve, consuming resources that could have fueled
expansion. Desalination plants generate the credits that fund construction.
Laboratories produce the science needed to upgrade infrastructure.

The underwater setting justifies the game's most important constraint: extreme
isolation. Each player builds on their own personal board, expanding outward
from a single starting dome. There is no shared map to contest — your
underwater nation is yours alone. This thematic framing explains why
interaction flows through the shared action board and card market rather than
through territorial conflict. You are not fighting over the same patch of ocean
floor; you are competing for the same limited pool of engineering expertise,
political influence, and technological breakthroughs represented by the central
action slots and card decks. The metropolis tiles — massive upgraded city
structures positioned at the far reaches of your player board — represent the
aspirational endgame of colonization: not just survival, but the construction
of true underwater civilizations worthy of permanent habitation.

### Core Loop

The game spans ten rounds divided into three eras, with production phases
occurring after rounds 4, 7, and 10. Each round follows a simple but
consequential structure:

1. CARD MANAGEMENT — Players begin each round with a hand of cards (starting
   the game with 3 after drafting from 6). At the end of each turn, the player
   draws 1 card from the current era's deck. At the transition between eras,
   players draw 3 cards from the new era deck, add them to any remaining cards,
   and discard down to 3. Era I cards tend toward basic resource generation,
   Era II cards offer stronger engine-building effects, and Era III cards
   emphasize end-game scoring and powerful late-game actions.

2. ACTION PHASE — Players take turns placing one of their three action markers
   on an unoccupied slot on the central action board, simultaneously playing
   one card from their hand. The board contains roughly 21 action slots divided
   into three color groups — green, red, and yellow. If the played card's color
   matches the slot's color, the player executes both the slot's action and the
   card's effect. If the colors do not match, the card is discarded for no
   benefit and only the slot action is performed. Each player takes exactly
   three turns per round — one per action marker — making every placement a
   high-stakes decision.

3. PRODUCTION PHASE (after rounds 4, 7, and 10) — All connected infrastructure
   produces simultaneously. Farms generate kelp, desalination plants generate
   credits, laboratories generate steelplast. Upgraded structures produce bonus
   resources or victory points. Symbiotic cities generate 2 VP each. Tunnels
   produce 1 credit each (upgraded: +1 VP). Then each connected city consumes
   1 kelp to feed its population — any shortfall must be covered from reserves
   or penalized. Production cards in players' tableaus activate during this
   phase, layering additional output onto the network's base production.

4. CLEANUP — Action markers are returned, the round advances, and in the final
   round of each era the card deck transitions to the next era's cards.

The fundamental rhythm is: draw cards, commit three actions that blend slot
selection with card play, then wait for the next production phase to harvest
returns from your expanding network. The ten-round structure with only three
production phases means players must plan infrastructure investments several
rounds ahead of their payoff — cities and buildings constructed in round 5
produce for the first time in round 7, making the timing of construction a
critical strategic dimension.

### Key Design Patterns

DUAL-ACTION COLOR MATCHING: The defining innovation of Underwater Cities is its
fusion of worker placement and hand management through the color-matching
system. Every turn requires two simultaneous decisions: which action slot do I
need, and which card do I want to play? When colors align, the player executes
both — a powerful dual action that can generate resources, build structures,
and advance on tracks in a single turn. When colors clash, the player must
choose: take the action I need and waste a card, or play the card I want on a
suboptimal slot. The design inverts the typical power balance — green cards
offer the strongest effects but green action slots are the weakest, while
yellow slots provide the best actions but yellow cards are the least impactful.
This asymmetric color valuation ensures there is no dominant strategy; every
color combination involves genuine tradeoffs. The system also means that
"better players tend to be luckier" — skillful hand management and flexible
planning allow experienced players to match colors more frequently, not through
chance, but through disciplined card draw timing and willingness to sacrifice
mismatched cards for critical actions.

THREE-ERA CARD PROGRESSION: The game's three era decks create a structural
narrative arc in the card economy. Era I cards provide foundational resource
generation and basic building effects — the tools for establishing your first
cities and tunnels. Era II cards introduce stronger engine-building components,
production multipliers, and more powerful permanent effects that reward players
who have built sufficient infrastructure to exploit them. Era III cards
emphasize end-game scoring bonuses, metropolis-enabling effects, and high-value
actions that pay off only if the player's network is mature enough to leverage
them. This progression means the card market evolves alongside the players'
networks — early draws that seem underwhelming become essential scaffolding,
while late-game cards that seem overpowered require a developed tableau to
activate. The era transition also creates information asymmetry: players who
remember which cards remain in the current era's deck can estimate the
probability of drawing specific effects, adding a layer of calculated risk to
every card draw decision.

NETWORK CONNECTIVITY AS ACTIVATION GATE: Nothing in Underwater Cities functions
unless it is connected. Buildings adjacent to disconnected cities produce
nothing. Cities without tunnels linking them back to the starting dome score
reduced points. Metropolis tiles at the board's periphery demand complete
tunnel connections before they yield their substantial bonuses. This
connectivity requirement transforms the personal player board from a passive
scoring tableau into an active spatial puzzle. Players must decide not just
what to build, but in what order — a kelp farm built next to an unconnected
city is a dead investment until the tunnel linking that city comes online.
The network topology also creates cascading value: each new tunnel potentially
activates multiple buildings and cities simultaneously, meaning that a single
construction action can unlock an entire section of dormant infrastructure.
This gating mechanism rewards deliberate expansion planning and punishes
scattered, opportunistic building.

TIGHT THREE-ACTION ECONOMY: With only three actions per round across ten
rounds, each player takes exactly thirty actions in the entire game. Thirty
actions to build cities, construct tunnels, erect buildings, upgrade
structures, acquire special cards, advance on the federation track, and
accomplish everything else the game demands. This extreme compression is the
engine of tension. In most worker-placement games, the scarcity comes from
contested action spaces; in Underwater Cities, the scarcity is existential —
even with no opponents, thirty actions would feel insufficient. The three-
action limit also interacts with the color-matching system to create what
amounts to a variable action-efficiency mechanism: a matched-color turn
effectively gives you two actions for the price of one, while a mismatched
turn wastes half its potential. Over a full game, the gap between a player who
matches colors 70% of the time and one who matches 50% is equivalent to
several bonus actions — a decisive advantage extracted not from the board
state but from hand management discipline.

PERSONAL BOARD AS ASYMMETRIC PUZZLE: Each player's double-sided board defines
the topology of their underwater nation. The basic side provides a symmetric
starting position, but the advanced side introduces genuine asymmetry —
different city locations, variable building site costs, bonus rewards for
specific construction patterns, and altered paths to the metropolis positions.
The advanced boards push players toward distinct strategic profiles: one board
might incentivize dense clusters of upgraded buildings around a few cities,
while another rewards sprawling networks that connect distant metropolises
quickly. This asymmetry operates at a different level than typical variable
player powers — it does not give players different abilities but different
spatial constraints, meaning the same card and the same action slot can have
very different strategic values depending on which board you are playing.

METROPOLIS AS ASPIRATIONAL ENDGAME: The metropolis tiles positioned at the
far corners of each player board represent the game's highest-value scoring
opportunities — but they demand significant infrastructure investment to reach
and activate. Brown metropolises score variable VP (up to 12 points) based on
their specific tile, but only if fully connected to the network via completed
tunnels. Blue metropolises provide immediate effects and production bonuses
upon connection. Reaching a metropolis requires building a chain of cities and
tunnels across the board, which means committing resources to expansion rather
than optimization of existing infrastructure. The metropolis system creates a
strategic fork: invest heavily in reaching high-value peripheral positions, or
focus on densely developing a compact network of fully upgraded cities near
your starting dome. Neither strategy dominates — the optimal balance depends on
your board layout, the cards you draw, and what your opponents' progress
demands of your pacing.

FIVE-TYPE CARD TAXONOMY: The card system divides effects into five functional
categories — instant, permanent, action, production, and end-scoring — each
creating different temporal value profiles. Instant cards provide immediate
resources or construction, useful for tactical flexibility. Permanent cards
offer ongoing abilities that compound over many rounds, rewarding early
acquisition. Action cards (limited to four in your tableau, refreshed each
production phase) provide powerful repeatable effects but consume scarce
tableau space. Production cards activate only during the three production
phases, making them worthless early but extremely valuable once a player's
network is producing at full capacity. End-scoring cards pay off only at game
end, requiring faith that the conditions they reward will be met. This taxonomy
forces players to manage not just which cards to play, but when different card
types yield maximum returns — a production card played in round 2 generates
value across all three production phases, while the same card played in round 8
activates only once.

FEDERATION TRACK AS TEMPO REGULATOR: The federation track serves a dual
mechanical purpose: it provides incremental bonuses as players advance, and it
determines turn order for subsequent rounds. In a game where action slots are
claimed on a first-come basis and color matching depends on available options,
going first is a significant advantage — you have the widest selection of
unoccupied slots to match against your hand. The federation track thus
converts a seemingly minor positional advancement into a tempo weapon. Players
who invest in federation advancement sacrifice immediate construction progress
for the right to choose first in future rounds, creating a classic
present-versus-future tradeoff. The track's bonus spaces also provide
resources, VP, or special abilities, meaning advancement is never purely about
turn order — it is a parallel scoring and resource engine that competes for
attention against the primary network-building game.

### What Makes It #49

Underwater Cities' design achievement is the color-matching system that fuses
worker placement and hand management into a single inseparable decision. Most
hybrid designs layer mechanics on top of each other — you do the worker
placement part, then the hand management part. Suchý's innovation is that
these two systems are the same action: you cannot place a worker without
playing a card, and you cannot play a card without choosing a slot. This
fusion creates a decision space that is richer than either mechanic alone,
because the quality of any given turn depends not on the action or the card in
isolation but on the interaction between them. The result is a game where hand
composition shapes strategic possibility — the same board state feels entirely
different depending on whether you are holding three green cards or a mixed
hand, and skilled play consists largely of engineering situations where your
hand and the available slots align.

The thirty-action ceiling and three-production-phase rhythm give Underwater
Cities a distinctive cadence among heavy Euros. Where many engine-builders
allow players to gradually accelerate their output over many rounds, Suchý's
design compresses the productive payoff into just three discrete moments.
Everything between production phases is investment; the production phases
themselves are the only harvest. This compression forces players to think in
terms of infrastructure-per-production-cycle rather than incremental
optimization, and it means that buildings constructed even one round too late
miss an entire production phase — a penalty equivalent to losing a third of
that structure's lifetime output. The game's network connectivity requirement
amplifies this temporal pressure: it is not enough to build the right things,
you must build them in the right order and connect them before the production
clock ticks. This interplay between the color-matching action system, the
network spatial puzzle, and the era-driven tempo creates a design where every
one of those thirty actions carries genuine weight.
