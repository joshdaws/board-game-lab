## Design Notes

Designers: Robert Dougherty, Darwin Kastle. Published by Wise Wizard Games (2014).

### Theme Integration

Star Realms places two players as rival star commanders building space
fleets from a shared trade row of available ships and bases. The science
fiction setting serves the deckbuilding mechanic well: the idea of
recruiting ships, constructing bases, and amassing trade wealth to fund
military operations maps naturally onto the core loop of buying cards to
improve your deck. The four factions -- Trade Federation, The Blobs, Star
Empire, and Machine Cult -- each embody a distinct strategic identity that
corresponds to their thematic role in the universe. The Trade Federation
generates authority (health) and trade income, reflecting a mercantile
civilization. The Blobs are aggressive alien swarm attackers, dealing raw
combat damage. The Star Empire focuses on card draw and forcing opponent
discards, representing an authoritarian power that controls information.
The Machine Cult specializes in deck thinning by scrapping cards,
embodying a technological civilization that values efficiency and
optimization over raw accumulation.

These faction identities are not merely flavor -- they are the game's
primary strategic axis. Each faction's mechanical identity creates a
distinct play style, and the ally ability system (where playing multiple
cards from the same faction triggers bonus effects) rewards commitment to
a faction strategy. The trade row is a shared galactic marketplace where
both players compete for the same ships and bases, creating a theme of
rival empires racing to recruit from a contested pool of available assets.
The direct combat model -- you reduce your opponent's authority to zero to
win -- gives the space battle theme real stakes that many deckbuilders
lack, where victory is often determined by point totals rather than direct
confrontation.

The base cards deserve special thematic note. Unlike ships, which are
played and discarded each turn, bases remain in play and provide ongoing
effects until the opponent destroys them. This creates a spatial metaphor
within the deckbuilder: bases are permanent installations that anchor your
strategy, while ships are transient forces deployed each turn. The
distinction between outposts (which must be destroyed before the player
can be attacked) and regular bases (which can be bypassed) maps onto the
military concept of fortified positions versus support infrastructure.
The result is a deckbuilder that feels like a space battle rather than
an economic engine, despite sharing the same fundamental buy-and-play
loop as its genre peers.

### Core Loop

Star Realms is played in alternating turns with no fixed round structure.
The game ends when one player's authority (starting at 50) is reduced to
zero. Each turn follows this sequence:

1. PLAY CARDS - Play any or all cards from your hand into your play area.
   Each card provides some combination of Trade (currency for buying),
   Combat (damage dealt to the opponent), and special abilities. Ships are
   played, used, and discarded at end of turn. Bases remain in play across
   turns, providing their effects each turn until destroyed.

2. ALLY ABILITIES - After playing cards, check for faction alignment. If
   you have two or more cards of the same faction in play (including
   bases from previous turns), each card of that faction with an ally
   ability triggers its bonus effect. Ally abilities are typically more
   powerful than base effects, rewarding faction concentration.

3. SCRAP ABILITIES - Some cards have a one-time scrap ability that can
   be triggered by permanently removing the card from the game. Scrapping
   is voluntary and can be done at any point during the turn. Scrapping
   thins your deck, removing weak starter cards or spent utility cards
   to increase the density of powerful cards in future draws.

4. COMBAT ALLOCATION - Allocate accumulated combat to destroy opponent
   bases (each base has a defense value) and/or deal damage directly to
   the opponent's authority. Outpost bases must be destroyed before direct
   authority damage can be dealt; regular bases can be bypassed. Destroyed
   bases go to the opponent's discard pile and will eventually return to
   their deck.

5. TRADE ROW PURCHASING - Spend accumulated trade to buy cards from the
   shared trade row of five face-up cards. Purchased cards go to your
   discard pile and will be shuffled into your deck when your draw pile
   is exhausted. You may also spend two trade to acquire an Explorer from
   the unlimited Explorer pile -- a basic card providing two trade and a
   scrap option for two combat.

6. DISCARD AND DRAW - Discard all played ships and remaining hand cards.
   Draw five new cards. If your draw pile is empty, shuffle your discard
   pile to form a new draw pile.

### Key Design Patterns

DIRECT COMBAT DECKBUILDING: Star Realms' most consequential design decision
is making player elimination the victory condition rather than victory
points. In Dominion and most deckbuilders, players build engines that
accumulate points, and interaction is limited to denial or marginal
interference. In Star Realms, your engine must actively destroy your
opponent. This transforms every purchasing decision from "what maximizes
my efficiency" to "what maximizes my damage output relative to my
opponent's defenses." The direct combat model creates urgency that point-
based deckbuilders lack: you cannot turtle indefinitely because your
opponent is simultaneously building an engine aimed at your authority. The
health-as-resource model (starting at 50, no way to exceed it via normal
means aside from the Trade Federation faction) means the game has a
natural arc from buildup to decisive engagement, usually resolving in
15-20 minutes.

FACTION ALLY SYSTEM: The ally mechanic rewards playing multiple cards of
the same faction by unlocking bonus abilities on each. This creates a
drafting tension in the trade row: do you buy the best card available
regardless of faction, or do you commit to a faction to unlock ally
synergies? Early-game purchases shape which factions become viable for
ally triggers later, meaning trade row decisions in the first few turns
have cascading strategic consequences. The ally system also introduces
hand-composition luck -- drawing three Machine Cult cards in one hand is
significantly more powerful than drawing one card from three different
factions -- which creates dramatic variance that matches the fast, combat-
oriented play experience. The system rewards strategic commitment while
introducing exciting peaks of power when faction-aligned hands come
together.

BASES AS PERSISTENT BOARD STATE: In a genre where most cards are played
and discarded each turn, bases break the pattern by remaining in play.
This creates a board state that persists between turns, giving the game
a spatial dimension that pure deckbuilders lack. Bases provide ongoing
effects (trade, combat, or ally triggers) and outpost bases serve as
shields that must be destroyed before the player takes direct damage.
The result is that players must consider not just their hand composition
but their tableau of active bases, and opponents must allocate combat
between base destruction and direct damage. Base management transforms
Star Realms from a pure hand-management game into one with meaningful
turn-to-turn board state decisions.

DECK THINNING AS STRATEGIC AXIS: The scrap mechanic allows players to
permanently remove cards from the game, including the weak starter cards
(Scouts and Vipers) that dilute deck quality. The Machine Cult faction
specializes in scrapping, but scrap abilities appear across factions.
Deck thinning is one of the most skill-testing decisions in the game:
scrapping a card sacrifices its immediate utility for long-term deck
quality improvement. Aggressive scrappers draw their powerful cards more
frequently but have fewer total cards in circulation, creating a density-
versus-volume tradeoff. The scrap mechanic provides a strategic axis
orthogonal to faction selection, meaning players must simultaneously
manage what they buy and what they remove.

TRADE ROW AS SHARED MARKET: Both players buy from the same five-card
trade row, creating direct competition for available cards. When a card
is purchased, a new one is drawn from the deck to replace it, meaning
the available options shift unpredictably. This shared market creates
hate-drafting opportunities: buying a card your opponent needs, even if
it is suboptimal for you, can be strategically correct. The trade row
also introduces a pacing dynamic -- expensive powerful cards may sit in
the row until someone can afford them, while cheap utility cards cycle
quickly. The shared market ensures that no two games present the same
purchasing options in the same order, and the tension between buying
what you need and denying what your opponent needs is the game's primary
interaction point outside of combat.

EXPLORER AS SAFETY VALVE: The unlimited Explorer supply (2 trade, scrap
for 2 combat) ensures that players always have a purchasing option
regardless of what appears in the trade row. This prevents the frustrating
scenario where all available cards are too expensive or strategically
irrelevant. Explorers also serve as efficient scrap fodder -- their two-
combat scrap ability makes them useful even after they become
suboptimal draws. The Explorer design demonstrates a principle applicable
across deckbuilders: providing a guaranteed baseline purchase option
smooths out the variance of a random market without eliminating the
excitement of premium card availability.

### What Makes It #173

Star Realms demonstrates that the deckbuilding genre can support direct
player conflict in a compact, fast-playing format. By replacing victory
points with player elimination and introducing persistent bases, the game
creates an experience that feels more like a duel than an optimization
exercise. The faction ally system gives strategic depth to drafting
decisions, the scrap mechanic provides a skill-testing deck-management
axis, and the shared trade row creates constant interactive tension. At
a weight of 1.91 and a playtime of 20 minutes, Star Realms achieves a
remarkable density of meaningful decisions per minute of play. Its success
spawned an entire ecosystem of expansions and inspired numerous combat-
oriented deckbuilders, but the core set remains a masterclass in design
economy -- proving that a deckbuilder needs nothing more than a single
shared deck, two starting hands, and an authority track to deliver a
complete and deeply replayable competitive experience. The game earns its
rank by being the definitive answer to the question of whether deckbuilding
can be a head-to-head combat format.
