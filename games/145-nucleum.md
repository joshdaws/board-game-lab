## Design Notes

Designer: Simone Luciani & David Turczi. Published by Board&Dice (2023).

### Theme Integration

Nucleum is set in an alternate-history Saxony where the discovery of nucleum --- a
fictional crystalline energy source --- has accelerated the industrial revolution
beyond its historical trajectory. Players are industrialists competing to
electrify cities, build energy production chains, and fulfill government
contracts in a landscape where steam power has been supplanted by this new,
volatile energy source. The alternate-history framing is not incidental; it
provides the thematic license for the game's central spatial puzzle. Real
industrial revolution games are constrained by historical geography and
technology progressions. Nucleum's fictional energy source allows the
designers to create an industrial system built around energy networks, power
generation, and transmission infrastructure that would be anachronistic in a
purely historical setting but feel entirely natural in the game's speculative
context.

The energy production chain is the game's thematic spine. Players do not simply
accumulate abstract resources; they build mines to extract nucleum, construct
power plants to convert it into energy, lay transmission lines to distribute
that energy to cities, and urbanize those cities to generate income and fulfill
contracts. Each step in this chain has a physical presence on the board: mines
sit on nucleum deposits, power plants occupy dedicated spaces, transmission
lines connect across the map, and urbanized cities display the player's
infrastructure. The visual result is a board that gradually transforms from
empty countryside to an interconnected energy grid, providing the tangible
satisfaction of building something complex and functional.

The player board tile-placement system mirrors the macro-level network building
with a micro-level spatial optimization puzzle. Each action is taken by placing
a tile on your personal board, and the position of that tile relative to
previously placed tiles determines adjacency bonuses. This creates a dual
spatial game: on the central board you build outward, connecting cities and
energy infrastructure across Saxony's geography; on your personal board you
build inward, optimizing the arrangement of action tiles to maximize synergies.
The resonance between these two scales of spatial planning gives Nucleum its
distinctive cognitive texture --- you are simultaneously an infrastructure
planner and an efficiency engineer, and both puzzles demand attention.

### Core Loop

The game plays over a variable number of rounds, ending when a player reaches
a scoring threshold or a supply of key components is exhausted. Each player's
turn consists of a single main action determined by tile placement:

1. SELECT AND PLACE AN ACTION TILE --- Choose an action tile from your
   available supply and place it on your personal player board. The tile's
   position matters: adjacent tiles may provide bonuses, and the spatial
   arrangement of your board determines ongoing efficiency. Some tiles
   must be placed in specific orientations or zones. The placed tile
   determines which action you take this turn.

2. EXECUTE THE ACTION --- Based on the tile placed, perform one of the
   following:
   a) BUILD MINE --- Place a mine on a nucleum deposit on the central board.
      Mines extract nucleum resources that feed into the energy production
      chain. Mine placement may require paying resources and connecting
      to your existing network.
   b) BUILD POWER PLANT --- Construct a power plant that converts nucleum
      into energy. Power plants have different capacities and conversion
      efficiencies, creating a tech-tree-style progression from basic to
      advanced facilities.
   c) LAY TRANSMISSION LINES --- Connect locations on the board with energy
      transmission infrastructure. Transmission lines extend your network
      reach and enable energy delivery to distant cities.
   d) URBANIZE --- Develop a city by investing resources and energy into
      it, increasing its level and generating income. Urbanized cities
      are prerequisites for contract fulfillment.
   e) FULFILL CONTRACT --- Complete a government contract by meeting its
      requirements (specific city urbanization levels, energy production
      thresholds, or network connectivity). Fulfilled contracts provide
      victory points and end-game bonuses.
   f) RETRIEVE TILES --- Reclaim previously placed action tiles from your
      player board, resetting them for future use. The retrieval action
      is the game's tempo mechanism: you must periodically pause to
      recover your action capacity.

3. ADJACENCY BONUSES --- After executing your main action, check for
   adjacency bonuses on your player board. Tiles placed next to compatible
   tiles generate secondary effects: additional resources, free network
   extensions, income bonuses, or tech track advancement. The adjacency
   system means that tile placement sequencing across multiple turns
   creates compound benefits.

4. INCOME AND MAINTENANCE --- At designated intervals, players collect
   income from urbanized cities and energy production, then pay maintenance
   for infrastructure. The income-maintenance differential determines
   economic sustainability.

5. TECH TRACK ADVANCEMENT --- Certain actions and adjacency bonuses advance
   your position on technology tracks that unlock upgraded tile types,
   improved action efficiencies, and end-game scoring multipliers.
   Tech tracks represent the industrial progression from basic to
   advanced nucleum applications.

### Key Design Patterns

TILE PLACEMENT AS ACTION SYSTEM: Nucleum's defining innovation is using tile
placement on a personal board as the action selection mechanism. Rather than
choosing actions from a shared worker-placement board or playing cards from
hand, players physically place tiles whose position, orientation, and
adjacency determine both the action taken and the bonuses received. This
creates a two-dimensional planning problem absent from most action selection
systems: you are not simply choosing what to do but where to do it, and
"where" has persistent consequences because tiles remain on your board until
retrieved. The spatial action system transforms the personal player board
from a passive tracking device into an active strategic landscape that
evolves across the game.

ACTION RETRIEVAL AS TEMPO CONTROL: Placed tiles remain on your board until
you spend an action to retrieve them, creating a rhythm of deployment and
recovery that structures the game's pacing. Early in the game, players have
many available tiles and can take diverse actions freely. As tiles accumulate
on the board, options narrow, forcing a retrieval turn that sacrifices
immediate productivity for future flexibility. This deployment-recovery
cycle creates a natural tempo that varies by player: aggressive players who
place tiles rapidly must retrieve more frequently, while conservative players
who place tiles selectively maintain flexibility but execute fewer actions.
The retrieval mechanism also interacts with adjacency bonuses --- retrieving
a tile removes its adjacency contribution, potentially reducing bonuses for
neighboring tiles.

DUAL-SCALE SPATIAL PLANNING: The game operates on two spatial scales
simultaneously. On the central board, players build outward, extending
energy networks across Saxony's geography. On the personal board, players
build inward, optimizing tile arrangements for adjacency bonuses. Both
scales demand spatial reasoning, but the cognitive challenges differ:
central board planning involves geographic strategy (where to build
infrastructure for maximum network coverage), while personal board planning
involves combinatorial optimization (how to arrange tiles for maximum
synergy). The interplay between scales means that an action's value depends
on both its contribution to your network on the central board and its
position on your personal board --- a single decision evaluated on two
different spatial axes.

ENERGY CHAIN AS SEQUENTIAL DEPENDENCY: The nucleum-to-energy-to-urbanization
production chain creates a strict sequential dependency: you cannot urbanize
without energy, cannot generate energy without power plants, and cannot fuel
power plants without mines. This dependency chain structures the game's
arc: early rounds focus on upstream infrastructure (mines, power plants),
mid-game shifts to transmission and urbanization, and late game targets
contract fulfillment. Players who skip chain steps find themselves unable
to execute downstream actions, while players who invest too heavily upstream
may lack the time to monetize their production capacity. The chain
creates a natural planning horizon: every mine built is an investment in
future energy production that may not pay off for several turns.

CONTRACT SYSTEM AS DIRECTED SCORING: Contracts provide specific scoring
targets that give direction to the open-ended network-building game. Each
contract specifies requirements (urbanization levels, connections, energy
thresholds) and rewards (VP, bonuses, end-game multipliers). The contract
market creates a metagame of evaluating which contracts align with your
existing infrastructure and which require costly diversions. Contracts also
create indirect player interaction: two players pursuing the same contract
may compete for the same city spaces or network routes, while players
targeting different contracts can coexist in the same geographic area
without conflict.

TECH TRACKS AS CAPABILITY SCALING: Technology tracks provide a progression
from basic to advanced industrial capabilities. Advancing on a tech track
unlocks improved tile types (more efficient power plants, longer transmission
lines, higher-capacity mines), upgraded action bonuses, and end-game
scoring multipliers. Tech advancement is not automatic; it requires
deliberate investment through specific actions and adjacency combos. Players
who prioritize tech advancement sacrifice short-term productivity for long-
term efficiency, while players who ignore tech tracks must compensate with
volume --- building more infrastructure to achieve what a technologically
advanced player achieves with less.

VARIABLE PLAYER POWERS AS STRATEGIC ASYMMETRY: Each player begins with
unique starting configurations --- different initial tiles, asymmetric player
boards, and specialized abilities that nudge strategies in different
directions. These asymmetries create divergent opening games from the first
turn and ensure that optimal tile placement sequences differ between players.
The variable powers are calibrated to be influential but not deterministic:
they suggest strategic directions without mandating them, allowing players
to lean into their asymmetric advantages or deliberately pursue contrarian
strategies.

### What Makes It #145

Nucleum earns its 4.19 weight through the interaction of its dual-scale spatial
systems rather than through rule density. The personal board tile placement
creates a micro-optimization puzzle that is genuinely novel --- no other heavy
Euro asks players to simultaneously plan network infrastructure on a shared
map and arrange action tiles on a personal spatial grid. The energy production
chain provides structural direction that prevents the analysis paralysis
common in heavy sandbox games: you know you need mines, power plants,
transmission, and urbanization; the question is where, when, and in what
proportion. The Luciani-Turczi collaboration brings together two designers
known for interlocking system design, and Nucleum is their most ambitious
joint creation: a game where the action system, the economic engine, the
network builder, and the tech tree are not separate modules bolted together
but facets of a unified spatial-economic puzzle. It represents the 2023 state
of the art in heavy Euro design: thematically grounded, mechanically
innovative, and strategically demanding without being procedurally complex.
