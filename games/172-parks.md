## Design Notes

Designer: Henry Audubon. Published by Keymaster Games (2019).

### Theme Integration

PARKS is built around the experience of hiking through America's national
parks, and it commits to this theme with an unusual degree of visual and
mechanical sincerity. The game's artwork is drawn from the Fifty-Nine Parks
Print Series, a collection of screen-printed posters celebrating national
parks, and each park card in the game features a unique illustration in
this distinctive style. This is not incidental art direction -- the visual
beauty of the park cards is itself a game mechanic. Players collect parks
not just for their point values but because the artwork makes them
desirable objects, creating an emotional dimension to card acquisition that
purely abstract scoring would not achieve. The game understands that
national parks are valued for their beauty, and it channels that value
through its components.

The trail mechanic expresses the hiking theme with elegant literalism.
Each season, players move their two hikers along a shared trail of
modular sites, collecting resources along the way. The trail changes every
season -- sites are shuffled and rearranged -- representing the shifting
landscape and conditions across spring, summer, fall, and winter. Resources
are thematically mapped: sunlight and water are the primary currencies,
representing the natural elements hikers depend on. Mountains, forests, and
wildlife tokens further connect the resource economy to the natural world.
The seasonal rotation of the trail means no two hikes feel identical, and
the changing availability of resources mirrors the real unpredictability of
nature across seasons.

The canteen and gear systems represent the preparation aspect of hiking.
Canteens are filled with water at specific trail sites and can be activated
for bonuses -- carrying water on a hike is both thematically intuitive and
mechanically meaningful. Gear cards provide persistent abilities that
modify how players interact with the trail, representing the equipment a
hiker accumulates over a season of outdoor adventures. The game treats the
national park experience holistically: it is not just about arriving at
a destination but about the journey, the preparation, and the resources
gathered along the way.

### Core Loop

The game is played over four seasons (rounds), each representing a hike
along a modular trail. Each season follows this structure:

1. TRAIL SETUP - Arrange the trail sites in a random order for the
   current season. The trail grows longer in later seasons (more sites
   in summer and fall than spring), representing longer and more
   ambitious hikes as the year progresses. Place resource tokens on
   each trail site according to its type.

2. HIKER MOVEMENT - On your turn, move one of your two hikers forward
   along the trail to any unoccupied site. You must always move forward --
   hikers cannot backtrack. When you land on a site, collect the resources
   shown (sunlight, water, mountains, forests, wildlife tokens) or
   perform the site's special action (buy gear, fill canteen, reserve a
   park, take photos). If a site is occupied by another player's hiker,
   you may only land there by spending one sunlight token to share the
   space, representing the courtesy of the trail. Each player alternates
   moving one hiker at a time until both hikers reach the trailhead at
   the end.

3. TRAILHEAD ACTIONS - When a hiker reaches the trailhead (the final
   space), the player may perform one of three actions:
   a) VISIT A PARK - Pay the resource cost shown on a park card to claim
      it. Parks require combinations of sunlight, water, mountains, forests,
      and wildlife tokens. Each park is worth victory points.
   b) BUY GEAR - Pay sunlight to acquire a gear card that provides a
      persistent ability for future seasons.
   c) RESERVE A PARK - Take a face-down park card from the available
      display to visit later, guaranteeing access to a specific park.

4. SEASON END - After all hikers have reached the trailhead, the season
   ends. Reveal the next season's weather pattern (an event card that
   modifies resource availability or trail rules for the upcoming season).
   Redistribute the trail sites for the new season. The first-player
   camera token passes to the player who has visited the fewest parks,
   providing a catch-up mechanism.

5. YEAR END SCORING - After four seasons, players score points from
   visited parks, collected photos, and end-game bonus objectives. The
   player with the most points wins.

### Key Design Patterns

SHARED TRAIL AS INDIRECT INTERACTION: The trail is a single shared path
that all players' hikers traverse, but it is not a race. Players move one
hiker at a time, always forward, and cannot land on occupied sites without
paying a sunlight cost. This creates a gentle but persistent interaction:
your movement choices affect which sites remain available for opponents,
and the sunlight cost for sharing discourages but does not prohibit
clustering. The interaction is indirect and non-confrontational, matching
the cooperative ethos of the hiking community while still creating meaningful
competition for resources and trail position. Players who rush ahead get
first pick of sites but may leave valuable resources for those behind them,
creating a natural pacing tension.

SEASONAL TRAIL MODULATION: The trail is rebuilt each season with a random
arrangement of sites, and the trail length increases in later seasons. This
achieves several design goals simultaneously: it prevents path memorization,
it creates a sense of progression as the year advances, and it ensures
each game has a unique resource landscape. The weather event cards add a
second layer of modulation by altering rules or resource availability for
specific seasons. The combined effect is that players must adapt their
strategy each season rather than executing a fixed plan, mirroring the
real unpredictability of outdoor conditions.

RESOURCE THEMATIC MAPPING: The five resource types -- sunlight, water,
mountains, forests, and wildlife -- are not arbitrary currencies but
thematic elements of the national park experience. Each park card
requires a specific combination that reflects its real-world character:
a desert park costs more sunlight, a Pacific Northwest park costs more
water and forests. This thematic mapping makes resource costs feel intuitive
rather than arbitrary, reducing cognitive load because players can often
guess what a park might cost before reading the card. The mapping also
creates natural resource scarcity patterns -- sunlight is more available in
summer, water in spring -- that reinforce the seasonal theme.

DUAL-HIKER TEMPO MANAGEMENT: Each player controls two hikers on the trail,
moving one per turn. This dual-worker system creates a pacing puzzle: do
you advance one hiker quickly to claim valuable sites, or move them in
tandem to maximize resource collection? Your trailing hiker determines when
you finish the season -- both must reach the trailhead before your season
ends. The two-hiker system also doubles the interaction surface, since each
player occupies two trail spaces at any given time, increasing the
likelihood of site-blocking and sunlight-spending decisions. Managing the
tempo of two hikers who cannot backtrack produces more interesting movement
decisions than a single token would.

CANTEEN AS PREPARATORY INVESTMENT: Canteens are acquired once and can be
filled with water at specific trail sites, then activated for bonus
resources at opportune moments. This system creates a prepare-then-execute
loop within the broader seasonal structure: you invest actions to fill your
canteen early on the trail, then spend that stored water for amplified
returns later. The canteen mechanic rewards forward planning and creates
interesting fill-versus-use timing decisions. It also thematically
reinforces the hiking preparation motif -- experienced hikers carry water
for the journey and use it strategically.

AESTHETIC VALUE AS DESIGN LEVER: PARKS deliberately leverages the visual
beauty of its park cards as a motivational force. Players often choose
parks based partly on which artwork they find most appealing, creating
an emotional attachment to their collection that transcends pure point
optimization. This is a conscious design choice: by making every park card
a miniature poster, the game ensures that visiting parks feels rewarding
regardless of strategic outcome. The visual distinctiveness of each card
also aids memory and recognition, reducing the cognitive overhead of
tracking available options. Beauty serves function.

FIRST-PLAYER CATCH-UP: The camera token (first player marker) passes each
season to the player who has visited the fewest parks. This is a simple
but effective catch-up mechanism that gives trailing players the advantage
of first choice on the new trail. It prevents runaway leaders without
punishing successful players, and it thematically frames the camera as
a tool for the less-experienced hiker who needs a head start. The
mechanism is subtle enough that it does not feel like artificial balancing,
yet it measurably tightens the competitive spread across games.

### What Makes It #172

PARKS succeeds by demonstrating that accessible weight and deep theme
integration are not opposing goals. At a weight of 2.12, it is one of the
lighter games in the upper BGG rankings, yet it consistently earns praise
for creating a satisfying strategic experience within its short playtime.
The shared trail mechanic delivers meaningful indirect interaction without
confrontation, the seasonal modulation provides replayability without
complexity, and the thematic resource mapping reduces cognitive load while
deepening immersion. The game's most distinctive achievement is proving
that visual beauty can function as a game mechanic -- the desire to collect
beautiful park cards drives player behavior as effectively as any point
incentive. PARKS occupies a rare design space as a game that appeals
equally to families, casual gamers, and enthusiasts, and its enduring
popularity reflects the broad appeal of a design that treats accessibility
as a design discipline rather than a compromise.
