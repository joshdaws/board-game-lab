## Design Notes

Designer: Bobby Hill. Published by Garphill Games (2021).

### Theme Integration

Hadrian's Wall places each player in the role of a Roman general tasked with
constructing a milecastle along the famous northern fortification that
Emperor Hadrian commissioned in AD 122. The wall was not merely a military
barrier but a complex administrative infrastructure --- part customs post,
part garrison, part symbol of Roman civilization's reach. Hill captures this
multifaceted reality by giving players not just a wall to build but an entire
settlement to manage: barracks for soldiers, civic buildings for citizens,
markets for trade, temples for worship, and entertainment venues to maintain
morale. The theme justifies the game's extraordinary breadth of tracks and
systems, because a real milecastle commander would indeed have juggled
military defense, civic administration, resource logistics, and cultural
development simultaneously.

The paper-and-pencil format enhances the thematic experience in unexpected
ways. Players fill in boxes on their personal score sheets, and the act of
marking progress along the wall, filling in guard towers, and checking off
civic buildings creates a tactile sense of construction that cardboard tokens
might not achieve. Each sheet represents the blueprint of a settlement that
grows through deliberate planning. The permanence of pen marks --- you cannot
undo a decision once marked --- mirrors the permanence of Roman stone
construction. The two-sheet system (a player sheet for the wall and defenses,
a fate sheet for civic development) captures the dual nature of the
historical enterprise: military infrastructure on one side, civilian life
on the other.

The game's six-year structure, with each round representing a year of
construction, creates a compressed historical timeline. Fate cards introduce
events --- raids by Picts, resource discoveries, imperial edicts --- that
force adaptation. The wall is not built in isolation; it responds to the
pressures of frontier life, and the game models this responsive construction
through its card-driven resource allocation.

### Core Loop

The game is played over six rounds (years). Each round, players simultaneously
select cards from a shared draft and allocate workers to build their personal
milecastle. The two score sheets --- the Player Sheet (wall, towers, fort,
road, cippi) and the Fate Sheet (markets, temples, theatres, barracks,
workshops) --- contain interconnected tracks and boxes that reward systematic
development.

1. CARD PHASE --- Each player receives a hand of cards from a shared deck.
   Players simultaneously draft cards using a closed drafting mechanism,
   selecting cards to keep and passing the remainder. Drafted cards provide
   two types of resources: workers (soldiers, civilians, builders) shown
   along the left edge, and trade goods or special abilities shown on the
   card face.

2. RESOURCE GATHERING --- Players total the workers provided by their
   drafted cards plus any bonuses from previously completed tracks on their
   sheets. A Fate card is also revealed each round, providing additional
   workers or resources to all players and sometimes introducing shared
   events. Workers come in three types: soldiers (used for military
   construction), civilians (used for civic development), and builders
   (wild, usable for either).

3. ALLOCATION PHASE --- This is the game's core activity. Players
   simultaneously assign their workers by marking boxes on their two sheets.
   Each track or building on the sheets requires specific worker types.
   Filling in a section of the wall requires soldiers. Constructing a
   market stall requires civilians. Completing a guard tower may require
   both. Players work through their sheets, spending workers one at a time
   to fill boxes. The order of allocation matters because many tracks unlock
   bonuses when completed --- additional workers, resources, or access to
   new areas of the sheets --- that can be immediately spent in the same
   round. Cascading bonuses are central to the experience: completing one
   track section triggers a reward that enables progress on another track,
   which triggers another reward, creating satisfying chains of productivity.

4. SCORING AND CLEANUP --- At the end of each round, certain tracks may
   produce ongoing benefits or contribute to end-game scoring conditions.
   Disdain tokens accumulate when defensive obligations are not met (wall
   sections left incomplete relative to the attack track), and each token
   subtracts from the final score. After six rounds, players total their
   scores across all tracks, subtract disdain penalties, and add bonuses
   from completed objectives. The highest score wins.

### Key Design Patterns

FLIP-AND-WRITE COMPLEXITY CEILING: Hadrian's Wall pushes the paper-and-
pencil genre to its maximum strategic density. Where most roll-and-write
or flip-and-write games feature a single sheet with a handful of
interconnected tracks, Hadrian's Wall uses two full-sized sheets containing
dozens of tracks, buildings, and scoring paths. The design proves that the
paper-and-pencil format can support weight-3+ strategic decision-making,
challenging the assumption that the genre is inherently lightweight. Hill
demonstrated that the format's constraint --- no physical components to
manipulate beyond a pen --- is liberating rather than limiting, because it
allows an information density that would be unmanageable with physical tokens
and boards.

CASCADING BONUS CHAINS: The game's deepest satisfaction comes from
triggering chains of bonuses. Completing a row on the wall unlocks a free
civilian worker, which can be used to fill a market stall, which provides
a resource that advances a track on the fate sheet, which grants another
worker. These cascading chains are the game's engine-building expression.
Players who plan their allocation order to maximize chain length within a
single round achieve dramatically more than those who allocate haphazardly.
The chain mechanic transforms the allocation phase from a simple resource-
spending exercise into a sequencing puzzle where the order of operations
determines total output.

TECH TREE PARALLELISM: The two sheets present multiple independent tech
tracks that players can advance along simultaneously. The wall track, the
fort track, the road track, the cippi track, the market track, the temple
track, the theatre track, the workshop track --- each progresses
independently, with its own cost structure and reward schedule. Players must
decide how to distribute limited workers across these parallel tracks,
and the optimal distribution changes based on drafted cards, fate card
events, and scoring objectives. The parallelism prevents any single dominant
strategy: spreading too thin means no track reaches its high-value
completion bonuses, while focusing too narrowly ignores scoring opportunities
on other tracks. The design rewards balanced development with a strategic
lean toward tracks whose bonuses feed your other investments.

SIMULTANEOUS PLAY AS SCALABILITY SOLUTION: Because all players work on
their personal sheets simultaneously, Hadrian's Wall scales from one to
six players with minimal increase in play time. The only shared activity
is the card draft, which occurs at round start. This design choice solves
the scaling problem that plagues most Euro games, where adding players
adds proportional downtime. A six-player game of Hadrian's Wall takes
roughly the same time as a two-player game, because the allocation phase
--- the game's longest activity --- is fully parallel. The simultaneous
structure also creates an interesting social dynamic: players are intensely
focused on their own sheets, occasionally glancing at others to gauge
relative progress, creating a shared but individual experience that
resembles a multiplayer puzzle more than a traditional competitive game.

DISDAIN AS NEGATIVE INCENTIVE: The disdain mechanism punishes players who
neglect military defense. As rounds progress, the attack track advances,
and players who have not built sufficient wall sections and guard towers
accumulate disdain tokens --- negative points at game end. This creates a
floor obligation: before pursuing high-value civic scoring paths, players
must invest enough in defense to avoid penalties. The design prevents
pure optimization along a single axis by imposing a mandatory minimum
investment in the wall itself. Thematically, this is elegant: you are
building a military fortification, and the game insists that military
readiness remains a priority regardless of how ambitious your civic plans
become.

OBJECTIVE CARDS AS STRATEGIC DIRECTION: At game start, each player receives
personal objective cards that reward specific combinations of track progress
--- for example, completing certain wall sections AND market stalls, or
reaching specific thresholds on both military and civic tracks. These
objectives provide direction without prescription: they suggest which
tracks to prioritize but do not dictate allocation decisions. Different
objective combinations create different strategic profiles across games,
ensuring that optimal play varies from session to session and preventing
the development of a single dominant strategy path through the sheets.

SOLO MODE AS DESIGN-FIRST CONSIDERATION: Unlike many games where solo
modes are afterthoughts, Hadrian's Wall was designed with solo play as
a primary mode. The solo experience is essentially identical to multiplayer
--- you draft against a simulated opponent, allocate workers, and score
against a target threshold. The game's simultaneous structure means that
the solo experience loses nothing in translation, because multiplayer
interaction was minimal to begin with. This design-first approach to solo
play reflects a broader trend in modern Euro design toward games that
function as personal optimization puzzles with a multiplayer wrapper.

### What Makes It #167

Hadrian's Wall stands as the most ambitious and strategically dense paper-
and-pencil game ever designed. Bobby Hill proved that the flip-and-write
format could support decision complexity rivaling traditional medium-weight
Euro games, and he did so with a thematic coherence that elevates the
experience beyond abstract optimization. The cascading bonus chains provide
the dopamine feedback of engine-building within a constrained format. The
simultaneous play structure makes it one of the most scalable designs in
the hobby, equally satisfying at one and six players. Its placement at
#167 reflects both the game's genuine strategic depth and the niche appeal
of its format --- not every player wants to spend an hour filling in boxes
on a score sheet, but those who do find one of the most rewarding
optimization experiences in modern board gaming.
