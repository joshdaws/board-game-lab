## Design Notes

Designers: Simone Luciani, Daniele Tascini. Published by Hans im Gluck /
Z-Man Games (2019).

### Theme Integration

Marco Polo II: In the Service of the Khan reimagines the Silk Road as a
network of opportunity, where players serve as envoys of the great Kublai
Khan, traveling between cities to establish trading posts, fulfill
contracts, and build an economic network spanning from Venice to Beijing.
The theme of service to the Khan is not merely narrative framing -- it
structures the game's objective hierarchy. Players are not free merchants
pursuing independent wealth; they are agents of a greater power, earning
favor through completing the Khan's objectives while simultaneously
building their own commercial infrastructure. The tension between
imperial service and personal ambition is encoded in the contract system,
where players must balance fulfilling assigned tasks (contracts that
provide resources and victory points) with investing in their own
trading post network (which generates ongoing income and strategic
positioning).

The travel system captures the essential challenge of Silk Road commerce:
moving goods and people across vast distances was enormously expensive and
logistically demanding. In Marco Polo II, travel between cities costs
resources -- camels, gold, and sometimes specific goods. These costs are
not arbitrary; they reflect the historical reality that the Silk Road's
greatest barrier was not bandits or politics but sheer distance and the
logistics of supply. Establishing a trading post in a distant city is a
major investment that pays off only if you can subsequently exploit that
city's unique action or connect it to a broader trade network. The game
makes physical movement across the map feel consequential and expensive,
which is exactly how Silk Road travel felt to the merchants who
undertook it.

The variable player powers represent different historical and fictional
envoys, each with a unique relationship to the game's systems. One
character might travel cheaply, another might gain extra dice, a third
might fulfill contracts more efficiently. These asymmetric abilities are
not minor bonuses -- they are game-warping advantages that fundamentally
alter strategic priorities. A character who can place trading posts
without physically traveling to cities approaches the map puzzle entirely
differently from one who must pay full travel costs. The asymmetric design
ensures that each character offers a genuinely distinct strategic
experience, reflecting the diverse skills and backgrounds that different
envoys would bring to Kublai Khan's court.

### Core Loop

The game is played over 5 rounds, each following this structure:

1. SETUP PHASE
   a. Each player rolls their personal dice pool (starting with 5 dice,
      expandable to 6). Die results are public information and determine
      the worker placement options available this round.
   b. The player with the lowest total die value becomes first player,
      providing a natural catch-up mechanism for poor rolls.
   c. New city bonus tiles and contract cards are refreshed for the round.

2. ACTION PHASE - Players take turns in order, placing one die per turn
   onto an action space. Actions include:

   a. KHAN'S FAVOR (value 1 required) - Take a contract card, camel, or
      gold. The universal fallback action that is always available.
   b. MARKETPLACE ACTIONS (3 slots, one per die value range) - Acquire
      resources. The available resources shift each round based on the
      market tile configuration.
   c. TRAVEL ACTION - Spend resources to move your figure along the
      Silk Road map from city to city. Each route between cities has a
      specific cost (combinations of camels, gold, and goods). When you
      arrive at a city, you may place a trading post if you have one
      available.
   d. CONTRACT ACTION - Take a new contract card from the available
      display. Contracts specify required goods and reward victory
      points, resources, or special abilities upon completion.
   e. CITY ACTIONS - If you have a trading post in a city, you may
      place a die on that city's unique action space. City actions
      provide powerful, location-specific benefits: resource conversion,
      bonus movement, contract fulfillment, extra dice, and more.
   f. GUILD ACTIONS - Specialized action spaces that offer trading,
      resource exchange, or scoring opportunities.

   WORKER PLACEMENT RULES: Multiple players can use the same action space,
   but subsequent players must pay coins equal to the lowest die already
   placed there. Higher-value dice provide no placement advantage but
   sometimes unlock better rewards within the action (e.g., marketplace
   offers scale with die value).

3. CITY BONUS COLLECTION - Players with trading posts in cities that
   have bonus tokens collect those bonuses (resources, camels, gold, or
   contract fulfillment opportunities).

4. CONTRACT FULFILLMENT - At any point during the action phase (as a
   free action), players may fulfill contracts by paying the required
   resources and collecting rewards. Fulfilled contracts are placed in
   the player's scoring area.

5. END OF ROUND - Unused dice are returned. Income is collected from
   trading posts and character abilities. The round marker advances.

After 5 rounds, final scoring adds: fulfilled contract points, trading
post network bonuses (connected cities), remaining resources converted
to points, objective card completion, and guild seal collection bonuses.
Highest total score wins.

### Key Design Patterns

DICE AS WORKERS WITH VALUE CONSTRAINTS: Marco Polo II's defining mechanism
is using rolled dice as worker placement tokens. Each die's value determines
where it can be placed and what benefits it provides. High-value dice
unlock better marketplace rewards but cost more when placed on occupied
spaces. Low-value dice are cheaper to contest but yield lesser rewards.
The random roll introduces variance at the start of each round, but the
strategic response to that variance -- choosing which dice to place where,
in what order, and whether to contest occupied spaces -- is where the
game's depth resides. The dice-as-workers system solves a classic worker
placement problem: it introduces meaningful variation between rounds
without requiring deck draws or event cards. Each round presents a
different tactical puzzle based on your dice configuration, preventing
the repetitive optimization that can plague traditional worker placement.

TRADING POST NETWORK AS EXPANDING ACTION SPACE: Each time a player
establishes a trading post in a new city, that city's unique action becomes
permanently available to them for the rest of the game. This means that
a player's action space literally expands as the game progresses. Early
rounds offer limited placement options; by round 4 or 5, a well-traveled
player might have access to 6 or 7 city-specific actions in addition to
the base actions. The expanding action space creates a compound growth
dynamic: early travel investments pay dividends across all subsequent
rounds, making the timing of trading post placement a critical strategic
decision. Players who invest heavily in travel early sacrifice immediate
scoring for a broader action palette later, while players who focus on
contracts early score quickly but operate from a narrower action base.

TRAVEL COST AS STRATEGIC COMMITMENT: Moving between cities requires
spending specific resources (camels, gold, and sometimes goods), and the
costs are substantial relative to round income. This makes each travel
decision a significant investment rather than a trivial action. The cost
structure creates geographic strategy: players must plan efficient routes
that minimize redundant travel, establish trading posts in cities whose
actions complement their strategy, and sometimes race to reach a
high-value city before opponents claim its bonus tiles. The travel cost
also interacts with the variable player powers -- characters who reduce
travel costs can pursue aggressive expansion strategies that would be
economically ruinous for others. The design principle is that expensive
movement creates spatial commitment: when traveling is costly, where you
go becomes a defining strategic choice rather than an incidental decision.

VARIABLE PLAYER POWERS AS STRATEGY WARPING: Each character in Marco
Polo II has a unique ability that fundamentally reshapes the game's
strategic landscape. One character starts with an extra die. Another can
use any die value as if it were a 6. A third can place trading posts
without physically traveling to cities. These are not marginal bonuses
but game-warping asymmetries that make each character play like a
different game. The radical asymmetry is balanced by the variable setup
and the competitive dynamics of the shared board -- a character who
excels at travel faces different optimal paths depending on which cities
are available and which opponents are competing for the same routes. The
design demonstrates that extreme asymmetry, when combined with sufficient
environmental variability, creates more interesting strategic diversity
than carefully balanced minor differences.

CONTRACTS AS DIRECTED SCORING: Contracts provide specific resource-to-
points conversion tasks that give players intermediate objectives within
the broader strategic landscape. Each contract specifies resources to
spend and rewards to gain, creating a clear optimization target. The
contract system prevents the common Euro game problem of decision
paralysis by giving players concrete short-term goals: acquire these
specific resources, deliver them for these specific rewards. Contracts
also create resource competition, as multiple players may need the same
goods, and the marketplace supply fluctuates across rounds. The design
function of contracts is to scaffold decision-making within an open-ended
strategic system, ensuring that even inexperienced players have actionable
goals each round.

CONTESTED PLACEMENT WITH COST ESCALATION: When multiple players want the
same action space, subsequent players must pay coins equal to the lowest
die already placed there. This cost escalation mechanism preserves player
agency (you can always use a contested space if you pay) while creating
economic competition for desirable actions. The mechanism is more elegant
than hard blocking (where subsequent players are simply locked out),
because it converts spatial competition into economic cost. A player who
desperately needs a specific action can always access it -- the question
is whether the cost is worth the benefit. The escalating cost also creates
positional advantages: the first player to place on a popular action pays
nothing, incentivizing early commitment to high-demand actions.

FIVE-ROUND ARC AS INVESTMENT TIMELINE: The fixed five-round structure
creates a clear investment timeline. Rounds 1-2 are infrastructure
building: traveling, placing trading posts, acquiring dice or abilities.
Rounds 3-4 are exploitation: using the expanded action space to generate
resources and fulfill contracts. Round 5 is a final sprint to convert
remaining resources into maximum points. This arc means that strategies
with different investment horizons compete directly: a player who scores
steadily from round 1 through contracts might finish with the same total
as one who invests heavily early and exploits aggressively late. The
five-round limit prevents indefinite engine-building by imposing a hard
deadline that forces players to convert infrastructure into points before
time runs out.

### What Makes It #200

Marco Polo II refines the dice-as-workers mechanism that made its
predecessor a modern classic, expanding the strategic landscape with a
richer map, more diverse city actions, and deeper asymmetric character
design. The trading post network transforms spatial exploration into
engine building, where every city visited permanently expands your
capabilities. The costly travel system makes geographic decisions feel
weighty and consequential. And the radical character asymmetry ensures
that each play offers a genuinely different strategic puzzle. Luciani
and Tascini demonstrate that a sequel can honor its predecessor's
innovations while pushing them further -- the dice-as-workers concept
gains new life when paired with an expanding action space and truly
game-warping player powers, creating a Euro that rewards both tactical
adaptation to dice rolls and long-term strategic planning across five
tightly contested rounds.
