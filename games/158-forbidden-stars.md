## Design Notes

Designers: Samuel Bailey, James Kniffen, Corey Konieczka. Published by
Fantasy Flight Games (2015).

### Theme Integration

Forbidden Stars is set in the Warhammer 40,000 universe, a grimdark science
fiction setting where four galactic factions wage perpetual war for control
of the Heresus Sector, a region of space recently opened by the retreat of
a massive warp storm. Each faction --- Space Marines, Chaos Space Marines,
Orks, and Eldar --- is mechanically distinct, with unique combat cards,
upgrade paths, and strategic personalities that reflect their lore. The
Space Marines are elite and resilient, Chaos corrupts and subverts, the
Orks overwhelm through numbers and aggression, and the Eldar strike
surgically and reposition with preternatural speed. The designers did not
simply apply a Warhammer skin over a generic area control game; they built
each faction's mechanisms to express its identity, so that playing the Orks
FEELS different from playing the Eldar in a way that goes beyond cosmetic
asymmetry.

The Heresus Sector itself is a modular board assembled from system tiles,
each containing multiple areas connected by routes. The warp storm's retreat
is represented mechanically: at the start of the game, warp storm tokens
block movement between certain systems, and these tokens are removed
gradually over the game's eight rounds, progressively opening the map and
forcing confrontation. This is a masterful thematic integration --- the
narrative premise (a warp storm retreating) is simultaneously the mechanical
pacing device (the map opens up as the game progresses). Early rounds are
characterized by local consolidation as factions build up within their
starting regions, and later rounds erupt into cross-sector warfare as the
barriers fall. The warp storm tokens transform what could be a static map
into a dynamic landscape that evolves in a way that is both thematically
coherent and strategically meaningful.

The combat system channels the Warhammer 40K emphasis on combined arms
warfare. Battles are resolved not through pure dice rolling but through a
card-driven combat system where each faction plays combat cards from their
unique deck to modify dice results, trigger special abilities, and counter
opponent plays. Combat unfolds over multiple rounds within a single battle,
creating narratives of momentum shifts, desperate gambits, and decisive
strikes. The combat cards are the primary upgrade path --- factions spend
resources between battles to purchase more powerful combat cards, gradually
expanding their tactical options. This makes each faction's combat deck a
persistent expression of how the player has developed their war machine
over the course of the game.

### Core Loop

The game is played over eight rounds. Each round consists of a planning
phase, an operations phase, and a refresh phase. Victory is achieved by
controlling objectives --- specific areas on the map where your faction's
objective tokens are located.

**Planning Phase:**

1. ORDER PLACEMENT --- Starting with the first player and proceeding
   clockwise, each player places one order token face-down on a system tile
   on the map. Players take turns placing orders until all players have
   placed four orders each. Order tokens are stacked --- later orders are
   placed on top of earlier ones, and orders resolve from top to bottom.
   The four order types are:
   - DEPLOY: Produce new units and structures in the system
   - STRATEGIZE: Purchase combat upgrade cards and event cards
   - DOMINATE: Claim objectives and generate resources from controlled areas
   - ADVANCE: Move units from the system into adjacent systems

2. STRATEGIC STACKING --- Because orders resolve top-down, a player can
   block an opponent's order by placing their own order on the same system
   tile on a later turn. The blocked order remains buried under the stack
   and will not resolve until the blocking order has been resolved first.
   This stacking mechanism is the game's primary strategic interaction
   during the planning phase: it allows bluffing, blocking, and temporal
   manipulation of the action order.

**Operations Phase:**

3. ORDER RESOLUTION --- Starting with the first player, each player
   resolves the topmost order token on any system tile. After resolving,
   the token is removed, potentially exposing the next order in the stack.
   Players alternate resolving one order at a time until all orders have
   been resolved or are blocked by unresolved orders above them.

4. DEPLOY ORDERS --- Place new units (infantry, vehicles, titans, orbital
   structures) in the system, limited by the faction's available unit pool
   and the structures present. Structures like factories and bastions
   enable advanced unit production and provide defensive bonuses.

5. ADVANCE ORDERS --- Move units from the ordered system into one or more
   adjacent systems. If units enter a system containing enemy forces, a
   combat is initiated. Movement is restricted by warp storm tokens, which
   block routes between systems until they are removed at the end of
   each round.

6. COMBAT RESOLUTION --- When forces from opposing factions occupy the
   same area, combat begins:
   a) Each player rolls dice based on their units' combat values. Dice
      produce hits, shields (defense), and morale results.
   b) Each player plays combat cards from their faction-specific deck.
      Cards modify dice results, add hits or shields, trigger special
      abilities, or force the opponent to discard results.
   c) Hits that exceed the defender's shields destroy units. Morale
      results can force retreats.
   d) Combat continues for up to three rounds unless one side is
      eliminated or retreats. Multiple rounds of card play create
      escalating tactical exchanges within a single battle.

7. STRATEGIZE ORDERS --- Purchase combat upgrade cards from the faction's
   available card row, adding them to the combat deck. Also purchase event
   cards that provide one-time strategic advantages.

8. DOMINATE ORDERS --- If you control all areas in the ordered system and
   one of your objective tokens is present, claim the objective. Also
   collect materiel (resources) from structures you control. The first
   player to claim their fourth objective wins immediately.

**Refresh Phase:**

9. WARP STORM RETREAT --- Remove one warp storm token from the map,
   opening new routes for movement.

10. ROUND ADVANCEMENT --- Pass the first player marker, reset event cards,
    and prepare for the next round. After eight rounds, the player with
    the most claimed objectives wins; ties broken by materiel.

### Key Design Patterns

ORDER STACKING AS STRATEGIC DEPTH: The order token stacking mechanism is
Forbidden Stars' most distinctive design contribution. By allowing players
to place orders on the same system tile, with resolution proceeding
top-down, the game creates a temporal puzzle where the sequence of actions
matters as much as the actions themselves. Placing an Advance order on a
system, then having an opponent stack a Deploy order on top of it, means
the opponent's reinforcements arrive before your attack. Players must
anticipate not just what opponents will do but when they will do it, and
the stacking mechanism rewards players who can read opponents' intentions
and disrupt their timing. The mechanism is also self-balancing: aggressive
blocking sacrifices flexibility (you are spending an order to deny an
opponent's action rather than to advance your own position), creating a
natural cost to interference.

FACTION ASYMMETRY AS IDENTITY ENGINE: Each of the four factions plays
fundamentally differently, not just in unit stats but in combat card
design and upgrade paths. The Space Marines have fewer units but each is
more powerful and resilient. The Orks field swarms of cheap units that
generate extra dice through sheer numbers. The Eldar have fragile units
but superior mobility and devastating precision strikes. Chaos corrupts
enemy units and manipulates the combat card exchange. This asymmetry means
that mastering one faction does not translate directly to mastering another,
and inter-faction matchups produce different strategic dynamics. The
asymmetry is calibrated to be flavorful rather than fragile --- no faction
has a dominant strategy, but each has a distinctive strategic personality.

CARD-DRIVEN COMBAT AS TACTICAL NARRATIVE: Combat in Forbidden Stars is
not a single die roll but a multi-round card-driven exchange that produces
genuine tactical narratives. Each round of combat involves rolling dice
AND playing cards, with cards able to modify results, cancel opponent
effects, and trigger faction-specific abilities. The multi-round structure
means that early rounds probe the opponent's defenses while later rounds
commit to decisive strikes. Players must manage their combat card hand
across the entire game, knowing that powerful cards played in one battle
are unavailable for the next. This transforms combat from a probabilistic
check into a strategic subsystem with bluffing, timing, and hand
management depth.

DECKBUILDING AS MILITARY DEVELOPMENT: The Strategize order allows players
to purchase combat upgrade cards that permanently improve their faction's
combat deck. Over the course of eight rounds, a faction's combat
capabilities evolve from a basic starting deck to a specialized war
machine. This deckbuilding subsystem creates a strategic arc: early
investment in combat upgrades pays dividends across many battles, while
late investment provides powerful cards but fewer opportunities to use
them. The upgrade path also creates divergent strategies within a single
faction --- a Space Marine player who invests in defensive cards plays
very differently from one who invests in offensive cards.

WARP STORM PACING AS ESCALATION ARCHITECTURE: The warp storm tokens that
block movement at the game's start and are progressively removed create a
natural three-act structure. Act one (rounds 1-3) is local: factions build
up within their starting regions, consolidating territory and investing in
upgrades. Act two (rounds 4-6) is expansive: newly opened routes allow
factions to probe neighboring territories and contest objectives. Act
three (rounds 7-8) is decisive: with most barriers removed, the map is
fully connected and the final push for objectives produces the game's
climactic confrontations. This pacing mechanism is elegant because it
requires no rules text beyond "remove a warp storm token at the end of
each round" --- the escalation emerges naturally from the map's progressive
opening.

OBJECTIVE PLACEMENT AS STRATEGIC TENSION: Each faction's four objective
tokens are distributed across the map during setup, typically in locations
that are not adjacent to the faction's starting position. This means that
claiming objectives requires projecting military power across the map,
often through territory controlled by other factions. The objective
placement creates inherent conflict: your objectives are likely in or
near other players' territory, and theirs are likely near yours. This
eliminates the "turtling" problem that plagues many area control games ---
you cannot win by sitting in your starting position, because your
objectives are elsewhere.

MATERIEL AS OPPORTUNITY COST: Resources (materiel) are generated through
Dominate orders and spent on unit production, combat upgrades, and event
cards. Because Dominate orders consume one of your four orders per round,
generating resources requires sacrificing other actions. This creates a
persistent tension between economic development and military action ---
you need resources to build and upgrade, but spending orders on resource
generation means fewer orders for movement and combat. The opportunity
cost ensures that economic and military strategies are always in tension
rather than developing in parallel.

### What Makes It #158

Forbidden Stars is widely regarded as one of the finest strategy games
Fantasy Flight Games ever produced, and its discontinuation following the
end of the FFG-Games Workshop licensing relationship has only increased
its reputation. The order stacking mechanism alone would justify the
game's place in the design canon --- it solved the simultaneous action
selection problem in a way that rewards reading opponents without
requiring hidden information. Combined with the faction asymmetry, the
card-driven combat system, and the warp storm pacing mechanism, the result
is a game that delivers the scale and drama of a grand strategy wargame in
a focused eight-round structure. At 3.84 weight, it demands commitment,
but every mechanism earns its complexity by contributing to a strategic
experience that no simpler game replicates. Its ranking at #158 endures a
decade after publication and years after going out of print, a testament to
design quality that transcends availability.
