## Design Notes

Designer: Kevin Riley. Published by Action Phase Games / Indie Boards & Cards (2017).

### Theme Integration

Aeon's End: War Eternal is set in the dying remnants of a world consumed by
a cataclysm called The Void. The last surviving enclave of humanity,
Gravehold, shelters behind magical barriers while breach mages — sorcerers
who channel The Void's own energy — defend against Nemeses, colossal
entities that emerge to destroy what remains. The post-apocalyptic fantasy
setting does meaningful work: it justifies the cooperative structure (mages
must work together against existential threats), the deckbuilding mechanism
(mages grow stronger by absorbing and repurposing dark energy), and the
breach system (magical portals that mages force open to channel increasingly
powerful spells). The desperation of the setting explains why mages start
weak and must build their power during the fight — they are improvising
with whatever resources they can scavenge.

The Nemesis system is the theme's strongest mechanical expression. Each
Nemesis is not a generic boss but a distinct entity with a unique attack
deck, special abilities, and behavioral patterns. Umbra spawns minions that
shield it while draining Gravehold's health. Brama corrupts the spell
market, making purchased cards dangerous. The Gate Witch manipulates the
turn order deck to give itself consecutive turns. Each Nemesis forces the
players to adapt their strategy fundamentally — what works against one may
be suicidal against another. The Nemesis design captures the narrative
fantasy of facing incomprehensible eldritch threats that each demand a
different form of heroism.

War Eternal is a standalone sequel that introduces new mages, Nemeses, and
card pools while maintaining full compatibility with the original Aeon's End.
The new mages push design boundaries: Mist is built around charge
manipulation, spending and gaining charges to fuel abilities. Remnant draws
power from the destroyed cards in the Nemesis discard pile. These designs
reflect the series' evolving confidence in its core mechanism — the
no-shuffle deckbuilder — by creating characters whose abilities specifically
exploit the predictability of deck ordering. The expansion mentality is baked
into the system's architecture: every new box adds orthogonal content (new
mages, Nemeses, market cards) without changing the rules, a modular design
philosophy that has proven commercially and mechanically durable.

### Core Loop

Players cooperatively fight a single Nemesis over approximately 30-45
minutes. The game alternates between player turns and Nemesis turns, with
the order determined by a randomized turn order deck.

**Setup:**

1. Each player selects a mage with unique starting deck, health, breaches,
   and special ability. Mages have 4 breaches (portals for playing spells),
   some starting open and others requiring gems to pry open.
2. Select a Nemesis and set up its unique health pool, special rules, and
   shuffled Nemesis deck.
3. Create the shared supply: 9 piles of market cards (gems for economy,
   relics for utility, spells for damage). Players choose these piles
   during setup, customizing the available tools.
4. Shuffle the turn order deck: 2 player turn order cards and 2 Nemesis
   turn order cards (adjusted by player count). This deck determines who
   acts each turn.

**Turn Order Resolution:**

1. Reveal the top card of the turn order deck.
2. If it is a player card, any one player takes a full turn.
3. If it is a Nemesis card, the Nemesis activates.
4. When the turn order deck is empty, shuffle all turn order cards and
   form a new turn order deck.

**Player Turn:**

1. CASTING PHASE — Resolve any prepped spells. Spells are placed face-up
   on open breaches during a prior turn and resolve at the start of your
   next turn. Most spells deal damage to the Nemesis or its minions.
2. MAIN PHASE — Perform any number of actions in any order:
   a) Play gem cards from hand for Aether (currency).
   b) Purchase cards from the supply using Aether — gems, relics, or spells.
      Purchased cards go to your discard pile.
   c) Open breaches by paying their escalating Aether cost. Opening breaches
      in sequence is cheaper; skipping to higher breaches costs more.
   d) Prep spells from hand onto open breaches (face-up, resolve next turn).
   e) Activate relics from hand for immediate effects.
   f) Use your mage's special ability (once per game or charge-based).
3. DISCARD PHASE — Place remaining hand cards on your discard pile IN THE
   ORDER YOU CHOOSE. This is the critical decision — the order you discard
   determines the order you draw them in the future, since the deck is
   never shuffled.
4. Draw 5 new cards. If your draw pile is empty, flip your discard pile
   face down as your new draw pile (no shuffle).

**Nemesis Turn:**

1. Resolve any persistent powers already in play (ongoing harmful effects).
2. Draw and resolve the top card of the Nemesis deck: attacks target
   specific players, Gravehold, or create persistent effects. Some cards
   are Power cards that charge up over multiple turns before unleashing
   devastating effects.
3. Perform the Nemesis's unique "Unleash" ability if indicated.

**Win/Loss:** Players win by reducing the Nemesis to 0 health. Players lose
if Gravehold reaches 0 health or if all mages are exhausted (0 health).

### Key Design Patterns

NO-SHUFFLE DECKBUILDING: The defining innovation of the Aeon's End system.
When your draw pile is empty, you flip your discard pile to form a new draw
pile — no shuffling. This means the order you discard cards determines the
order you draw them. In a traditional deckbuilder (Dominion, Star Realms),
shuffling randomizes your deck, making hand composition unpredictable. In
Aeon's End, hand composition is controllable. If you discard your strongest
spell last, you know it will be on top of your next draw pile and therefore
in your next hand. This single rule change transforms the deckbuilder genre
from a probability management game into a sequencing puzzle. Players plan
not just which cards to buy but exactly when each card will appear in their
hand. The no-shuffle rule also dramatically reduces downtime — no pausing
to shuffle a growing deck — and it makes the discard phase the most
strategically important moment of each turn.

VARIABLE TURN ORDER AS UNCERTAINTY ENGINE: The turn order deck is shuffled
each cycle, randomizing whether players or the Nemesis act first. This
prevents perfect planning: you cannot guarantee that your carefully prepped
spell will resolve before the Nemesis attacks. If two Nemesis cards come up
consecutively, the team may face a devastating double turn with no chance
to respond. If two player cards come up together, the team gets a powerful
window to coordinate. The randomized turn order is the primary source of
tension in a game that otherwise permits deep planning (thanks to the
no-shuffle mechanism). It models the chaos of combat: you can prepare
perfectly, but you cannot control when the enemy strikes.

BREACH SYSTEM AS PROGRESSIVE INVESTMENT: Each mage has four breaches
arranged in a row. The first one or two start open; the rest are closed.
Open breaches let you prep spells; closed breaches are useless. Opening
breaches costs Aether, and the cost escalates: the third breach is
significantly more expensive than the second, and the fourth is a major
investment. However, you can focus a closed breach by paying a partial cost
on one turn, then finishing on a later turn — or you can prep a spell to a
closed breach at reduced effectiveness. The breach system creates a
meaningful long-term investment decision: opening more breaches increases
your combat output dramatically (more spells per turn), but the Aether
spent on breaches is Aether not spent on acquiring better cards. The system
ensures that mages start weak and grow powerful, creating a natural
dramatic arc of increasing capability.

NEMESIS AS ASYMMETRIC PUZZLE: Each Nemesis has a unique health pool,
special rules, a tailored attack deck, and a signature Unleash ability.
The design commitment is that no two Nemeses play alike. Rageborne
charges headlong at players, dealing escalating damage. Hollow Crown
corrupts the card market and turns the players' own purchases against them.
Wayward One manipulates breach positions, disrupting spell preparation.
This asymmetric boss design means the players' strategy must adapt
completely to the Nemesis they face. The same mage lineup and market
selection that trivializes one Nemesis may be inadequate against another.
The Nemesis system is what gives Aeon's End its replay value — the
combinatorial space of mages, market configurations, and Nemeses produces
hundreds of distinct tactical scenarios.

COOPERATIVE HAND MANAGEMENT WITHOUT QUARTERBACKING: The variable turn
order mitigates the classic cooperative game problem of one experienced
player directing everyone else's turns. Because you do not know who will
act next, you cannot plan a perfect sequence of player actions. Each player
must make independent decisions about what to prep and when to buy, because
the turn order may not support the "optimal" collective plan. The mage
special abilities further reduce quarterbacking by giving each player
unique decision points that only they fully understand. The no-shuffle
mechanism also helps: each player's deck ordering is a private puzzle
that outsiders cannot easily optimize without deep knowledge of that
player's discard history.

MARKET CUSTOMIZATION AS STRATEGIC METAGAME: Before each game, players
collectively choose which 9 card piles to include in the supply. This
pre-game decision shapes the entire session. A market heavy on damage
spells supports aggressive strategies. A market with strong gems enables
rapid breach opening. A market with powerful relics provides utility and
flexibility. Against Nemeses that corrupt the market, players might choose
cards they are comfortable losing. This pre-game customization creates a
metagame layer — experienced groups develop market configurations tailored
to specific Nemesis matchups, and the strategic conversation begins before
the first card is drawn.

SPELL PREP TIMING AS COMBAT RHYTHM: Spells are not played immediately —
they are prepped to breaches on one turn and resolve at the start of
your next turn. This delayed execution creates a combat rhythm: you invest
this turn for payoff next turn. The delay also means spells can be wasted
if circumstances change (the target minion is killed by another player
before your spell resolves). The prep timing interacts with the variable
turn order to create genuine tension: you prep a powerful spell, but will
you get to cast it before the Nemesis acts? The delay is not a penalty —
it is the mechanism that makes combat feel like coordinated magical
preparation rather than instant gratification.

### What Makes It #149

Aeon's End: War Eternal earns its position by demonstrating that a single
rule change — eliminating the shuffle — can redefine an entire genre. The
no-shuffle mechanism transforms the deckbuilder from a game of probability
into a game of sequencing, and every other system in the design supports
this transformation. The variable turn order provides the uncertainty that
the no-shuffle mechanism removes from deck composition. The breach system
creates a satisfying investment arc. The Nemesis design ensures that
mastering the system requires adapting to fundamentally different
challenges. As a standalone sequel, War Eternal refines the original
Aeon's End formula with more ambitious mage designs and tighter Nemesis
balance. It stands as proof that cooperative deckbuilding can be both
deeply strategic and narratively tense — not through legacy mechanisms or
campaign structures, but through the pure mechanical innovation of
controlling your own deck's future.
