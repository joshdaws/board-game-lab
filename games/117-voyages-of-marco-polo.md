## Design Notes

Designer: Simone Luciani & Daniele Tascini. Published by Hans im Gluck (2015).

### Theme Integration

The Voyages of Marco Polo reimagines the Silk Road as an economic race where
players are associates of the famous Venetian merchant, traveling through the
Middle East and Central Asia to complete contracts and establish trading posts
in lucrative cities. The theme is expressed most concretely through the map:
a point-to-point network connecting cities from Venice to Beijing, with routes
requiring coin and camel payments that scale with distance. Travel is expensive
and slow, requiring dedicated dice and substantial resources, which mirrors the
historical reality that Silk Road journeys were multi-year commitments requiring
enormous capital. The goods system — gold, silk, pepper, and camels — maps onto
actual commodities that drove medieval East-West trade.

The character system is where the theme becomes most ambitious. Ten historical
and fictional characters each break a fundamental rule of the game in a
different way. Rashid ad-Din Sinan does not roll dice — he simply declares what
values he wants. Matteo Polo starts with a white die, giving him an extra action
each round. Berke Khan does not need to travel to place trading posts — he pays
camels to place them remotely. Mercator ex Tabriz can treat any good as any
other good. Each character represents a different merchant archetype: the
connected insider, the well-supplied expeditioner, the shrewd trader, the
politically powerful. The asymmetry is extreme by Euro game standards — each
character fundamentally alters which strategies are viable, forcing players to
adapt their approach to their specific identity rather than following a generic
optimal path.

The contract system grounds the abstraction in commercial reality. Each contract
specifies goods and camels required for delivery and rewards in victory points,
coins, camels, or goods. Contracts are not abstract tasks but trade deals —
you are assembling a shipment of silk, pepper, and camels to deliver for profit.
The completed contracts are stored in a drawer on your player board, a tactile
detail that reinforces the sense of accumulating commercial success.

### Core Loop

The game spans 5 rounds. Each round, players roll their 5 personal dice and
take turns placing dice on action spaces to perform actions immediately:

1. TAKE 5 COINS — Place exactly 1 die (value irrelevant) to take 5 coins from
   the supply. A reliable income action when cash is needed.
2. GO TO THE GRAND BAZAAR — Place 1-2 dice on one of four goods spaces (gold,
   silk, pepper, or camels). The value of the lowest die placed determines how
   many goods you receive (e.g., a die showing 4 on the silk space yields up
   to 4 silk). Goods are stored in your warehouse on your player board.
3. SEEK THE KHAN'S FAVOR — Place exactly 1 die on the next available space
   (left to right). Take 1 good of your choice plus 2 camels. Each subsequent
   player must place a die of equal or higher value than the previous one,
   creating an escalating cost structure.
4. TAKE A CONTRACT — Place exactly 1 die on the contracts space. The die value
   determines which contracts you may select (contracts are arranged in a row
   numbered 1-6). Take 1 or 2 contracts and place them on your player board's
   active contract spaces.
5. TRAVEL — Place exactly 2 dice. The lowest die value determines how many
   spaces you may move on the map. Pay coin costs shown on the travel action
   space, plus route-specific costs (coins or camels) for each connection
   used. When you stop at a small city, you must place a trading post and
   immediately receive the city's bonus. When you stop at a large city, you
   must place a trading post and may use the city's unique City card action
   on future turns.
6. CITY CARD ACTIONS — In cities where you have a trading post, place 1 die
   on the City card to use its unique ability. Die value typically determines
   how many times you can perform the action.

BONUS ACTIONS (available any time during your turn): Complete 1 contract (pay
required goods and camels, receive rewards), Take 3 coins, Reroll 1 die,
Adjust 1 die result by 1, or Take 1 black die (costs 3 camels). Completing
contracts is a bonus action, not a main action, meaning you can fulfill
deliveries alongside your regular dice placement.

After 5 rounds, final scoring adds: Goal card bonuses (2 per player, scored
for visiting specific cities), 1 VP per trading post on the board beyond the
8th, and Beijing bonuses for reaching the eastern capital.

### Key Design Patterns

DICE-AS-WORKERS WITH VALUE MATTERING: The game's central innovation is using
dice as workers whose pip values determine action strength. Higher values yield
more goods, more movement range, more contract options. This creates a natural
tension between wanting high dice (for powerful actions) and the cost of using
occupied spaces (which scales with the difference between your die and the
existing die). Unlike pure worker placement where all workers are equivalent,
dice workers create asymmetric valuation of the same action space across
players. A player who rolled a 6 values the Bazaar differently than a player
who rolled a 2. The bonus action to adjust a die by 1 or reroll becomes
genuinely strategic — a 3 that becomes a 4 might unlock a critical contract
or provide one more camel from the Khan.

OCCUPIED SPACE SURCHARGE INSTEAD OF BLOCKING: Traditional worker placement
games block opponents from occupied spaces. Marco Polo takes a different
approach: you may always use an occupied blue action space by paying coins
equal to the lowest die you are placing. Brown action spaces (limited capacity)
are truly blocked, but the core blue spaces remain accessible at a premium.
This eliminates the pure frustration of being shut out while preserving
meaningful interaction — the first player to take an action gets it free, while
followers pay. The cost scales with die value, creating an additional tension:
placing a low-value die is cheap to duplicate but yields weak results, while a
high-value die is expensive to duplicate but powerful. This mechanic keeps the
game competitive without creating the zero-sum blocking that can make worker
placement feel oppressive.

EXTREME ASYMMETRY VIA CHARACTER POWERS: Each of the ten characters violates a
core rule in a dramatic way that reshapes the entire strategic landscape.
Rashid ad-Din Sinan chooses his die values instead of rolling, eliminating luck
entirely but losing the bonus actions that manipulate dice (since he never
needs them). Kubilai Khan starts in Beijing rather than Venice, reversing the
travel dynamic entirely. Johannes Caprini earns VP for every new city visited
regardless of contracts. This level of asymmetry means that the "correct"
strategy is different for each character, and experienced players must evaluate
board state through the lens of their specific power. The asymmetry also
creates an implicit balancing mechanism: characters with powerful economic
abilities may struggle with travel, while travel-focused characters may lag in
contract completion. First-game recommendations assign specific characters to
reduce overwhelm.

CONTRACT ECONOMY AS VICTORY ENGINE: Contracts are the primary source of victory
points but also provide secondary rewards — coins, goods, camels, or additional
contracts — that fuel further economic activity. The tension between taking
contracts (which costs a die and provides no immediate resources) and acquiring
goods (which costs dice but is needed to complete contracts) creates a natural
economic rhythm. Completing a contract is a bonus action, meaning players can
chain contract completions within a single turn if they have accumulated enough
goods. The contract row refreshes as contracts are taken, and the sliding
mechanic — where remaining contracts shift left after one is removed — prevents
strategic hoarding of the deck.

TRAVEL AS HIGH-COST INVESTMENT: Moving on the map requires 2 dice (the most
expensive action by die count), coin payments to the travel space, and
additional route costs in coins or camels. This makes travel a significant
commitment that competes directly with economic actions. But the rewards
justify the cost: small cities provide immediate bonuses (goods, camels, coins)
that recur at the start of each subsequent round, while large cities unlock
exclusive City card actions that provide unique capabilities. Trading post
placement also scores through Goal cards and the 8th/9th trading post VP
bonuses. The question of when and where to travel — and how many dice to dedicate
to movement versus economic actions — is the game's central strategic fork.

FIVE-ROUND COMPRESSION: With only 5 rounds and 5 dice per player per round,
every action must count. There is no room for exploratory early rounds or
recovery from mid-game mistakes. This compression amplifies the weight of every
die placement and makes the starting character choice genuinely consequential.
The tight round count also means that investments must pay off quickly — a
trading post placed in round 4 provides only 1-2 rounds of recurring income,
while one placed in round 1 generates compound returns over the full game.
This tempo pressure distinguishes Marco Polo from more forgiving Euro games
where engine building can unfold gradually.

GOAL CARDS AS HIDDEN DIRECTION: Each player receives 2 Goal cards at setup,
specifying cities that award bonus VP if the player has a trading post there
at game end. These hidden objectives create divergent travel priorities that
are not visible to opponents. Two players with different Goal cards may be
racing toward completely different regions of the map, and the inability to
read opponents' travel priorities introduces meaningful uncertainty into
competitive planning. Goal cards also prevent purely reactive play — you must
commit to a travel direction that serves your hidden objectives even when the
board state might suggest otherwise.

### What Makes It #117

The Voyages of Marco Polo succeeds by layering meaningful asymmetry onto a
tight, compressed Euro framework. The dice-as-workers system creates natural
variation each round — you must adapt your strategy to what you rolled, not
just to what you planned. The character powers are bold enough to feel genuinely
transformative rather than incremental, ensuring that no two players at the
same table are solving the same puzzle. The travel system creates genuine
geographic tension on a map that feels like a real network of trade routes
rather than an abstract victory point track. And the five-round time pressure
ensures that every decision carries weight — there is no room to waste actions.
The game finds an elegant balance between the deterministic planning that Euro
gamers value and the adaptive improvisation that dice introduce, with character
powers providing the strategic identity that ties everything together.
