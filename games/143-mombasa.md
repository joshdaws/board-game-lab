## Design Notes

Designer: Alexander Pfister. Published by eggertspiele / R&G / Pegasus Spiele (2015).

### Theme Integration

Mombasa is set during the colonial era of African trade, with players acting as
investors expanding the reach and value of four chartered trading companies ---
Mombasa, Cape Town, Saint-Louis, and Cairo. The theme is uncomfortable by
modern standards, and the designer has acknowledged this; the game explicitly
avoids depicting the human cost of colonial trade, focusing instead on the
abstract economics of investment and expansion. Mechanically, the trading
companies function as shared enterprises whose value rises as players jointly
expand their territory. Each company has a track on the board representing its
geographical spread, and when any player expands a company, all players who
hold shares in that company benefit from the increased value. This shared
investment structure creates the game's central strategic tension: expanding a
company helps all its shareholders, but your positional advantage depends on
holding more shares than your opponents.

The investment mechanism uses a stock-track system rather than discrete share
certificates. Each player has a separate track for each of the four companies,
and advancing along a track represents deeper investment. Higher positions on
the track yield more VP at game end, but the track advancement actions are
scarce and must be weighed against other priorities. The elegant consequence is
that players naturally specialize in two or three companies while ignoring or
minimally investing in the fourth, because track advancement is expensive and
spreading across all four dilutes your scoring potential. This organic
specialization creates player asymmetry from identical starting positions.

The bookkeeping track and diamond mining operations serve as scoring sub-games
that diversify the strategic landscape beyond pure company investment. The
bookkeeping track rewards set collection of specific card types, while diamond
mining provides a separate point engine. These side quests prevent the game
from collapsing into a single-axis optimization and reward players who can
balance multiple scoring avenues without losing focus on their primary
company investments.

### Core Loop

The game plays over seven rounds, each consisting of three phases:

1. PLANNING PHASE (Simultaneous) --- Each player selects cards from their hand
   and places them face-down in three slots corresponding to action columns on
   the central board. Cards are played simultaneously, meaning all players
   commit to their actions before seeing opponents' choices. Each slot can
   hold one or more cards, and the number and type of cards played into a
   slot determines the strength and nature of the actions available in that
   column. This simultaneous commitment is the game's foundational decision
   point: you must allocate your hand across three action areas without
   knowing how opponents are distributing theirs.

2. ACTION PHASE (Sequential) --- Starting with the first player and proceeding
   clockwise, players take turns executing actions. On each turn, a player
   resolves one action from one of their three columns, or passes. Actions
   include:
   a) COMPANY EXPANSION --- Place trading posts to expand a company's
      territorial reach. The card(s) played determine which company and how
      far it expands. Expansion increases the company's value for all
      investors.
   b) TRACK ADVANCEMENT --- Move your investment marker forward on a
      company's stock track, deepening your stake. Requires specific card
      combinations or resources.
   c) BOOKKEEPING --- Advance on the bookkeeping track by collecting and
      organizing specific card types. The bookkeeping track awards VP at
      set thresholds and provides bonus actions.
   d) DIAMOND MINING --- Invest in diamond operations for an alternative
      scoring path that operates independently from company investment.
   e) CARD ACQUISITION --- Draft new cards from the market display to
      improve your deck for future rounds.
   f) BONUS ACTIONS --- Various actions unlocked through bookkeeping
      progress or card abilities.

3. CARD RECOVERY PHASE --- This is Pfister's signature mechanism. After all
   actions are resolved, the cards played into each of the three slots are
   NOT returned to a single hand. Instead, each slot's cards are placed into
   a separate discard pile. Players have three discard piles, one for each
   slot. At the start of the next round, players pick up ONE of their three
   discard piles to form their new hand, leaving the other two discard piles
   in place. This means cards played this round are unavailable next round
   (unless you pick up the pile containing them) and rotate back into
   availability on a multi-round cycle.

After seven rounds, final scoring tallies investment track positions for each
company (multiplied by company value), bookkeeping track progress, diamond
mining VP, and end-game bonus tiles.

### Key Design Patterns

ACTION QUEUE WITH ROTATING DISCARD PILES: The three-discard-pile system is
Mombasa's defining innovation and Pfister's most influential mechanism design.
By forcing players to distribute played cards across three piles and then
recover only one pile per round, the game creates a rotating hand management
challenge where cards cycle through availability on a two-to-three round
cadence. A powerful card played into pile A this round will not return to your
hand until you choose to pick up pile A in a future round, but picking up
pile A means leaving piles B and C for yet another round. This creates a
planning horizon that extends well beyond the current round: experienced
players think two or three rounds ahead, sequencing their pile pickups to
ensure the right cards are available at the right time. The mechanism also
prevents hand-dumping --- you cannot simply play your best cards every round
because doing so means they all end up in the same pile and create feast-or-
famine cycles. Strategic distribution of card strength across piles is a
skill that separates novice and expert play.

SIMULTANEOUS COMMITMENT AS INFORMATION HIDING: The planning phase requires
all players to commit cards face-down before any actions resolve. This
simultaneous commitment serves two functions: it eliminates the analysis
paralysis of sequential play (all players plan in parallel, reducing
downtime) and it creates hidden information that drives bluffing and
reading. When you see an opponent pick up their discard pile containing
company expansion cards, you can infer their likely focus this round, but
you cannot know which column they will assign those cards to or how they
will split their other cards. The information is partial --- enough to
inform strategy but insufficient to calculate optimal responses.

SHARED COMPANY VALUE AS COOPERATIVE-COMPETITIVE TENSION: Expanding a
company benefits all players who hold shares, but the player with the
deepest investment (highest track position) benefits most. This creates a
nuanced cooperative-competitive dynamic: you want someone to expand the
companies you have invested in, but you prefer that someone else spend their
actions doing it. A player who expands a company they do not lead in is
effectively subsidizing the leader's score. This tension means that company
expansion decisions are never purely about your own position --- they are
calculations about relative advantage across all shareholders.

BOOKKEEPING TRACK AS PARALLEL OPTIMIZATION: The bookkeeping track operates
as a set-collection minigame running alongside the main company investment
game. It rewards collecting specific card types and organizing them
efficiently, providing VP thresholds and bonus actions that feed back into
the main game. The bookkeeping track ensures that card evaluation is never
one-dimensional: a card's value depends not only on its company expansion
or investment utility but also on whether it advances your bookkeeping
position. Players who ignore bookkeeping sacrifice a significant scoring
avenue, but pursuing it aggressively diverts attention and cards from
company investment. The balance between these parallel tracks is the game's
deepest strategic question.

CARD DRAFTING AS DECK EVOLUTION: Acquiring new cards from the market
improves your deck's quality over time, adding stronger company expansion
cards, more versatile action cards, or bookkeeping-efficient card types.
This deckbuilding element creates a long-arc strategy: early investments in
card quality compound across remaining rounds as better cards cycle through
your three-pile rotation more frequently. The draft also creates indirect
player interaction --- taking a card an opponent needs can be as strategic as
taking one you need yourself.

VARIABLE PLAYER POWERS AS STRATEGIC SEEDING: Starting bonuses and asymmetric
abilities give each player a gentle push toward different company investments
and strategic emphases. These initial asymmetries do not lock players into
specific strategies but create divergent opening positions that shape the
first few rounds of play. By mid-game, initial advantages matter less than
accumulated investments, but the seeding ensures that early rounds do not
produce identical play patterns.

WEIGHT-TO-ELEGANCE RATIO: At 3.89 weight, Mombasa is a heavy game, but its
rules overhead is remarkably low relative to its strategic depth. The core
systems --- simultaneous card placement, sequential action resolution, and
three-pile recovery --- can be explained in minutes. The depth comes from the
interaction between these simple systems: the pile rotation creates planning
horizons, the shared company values create social dynamics, and the
bookkeeping track creates multi-axis optimization. Pfister's design
philosophy, refined here and carried forward into Great Western Trail and
Maracaibo, is to generate complexity from mechanism interaction rather than
rule accumulation.

### What Makes It #143

Mombasa is the game where Alexander Pfister found his voice as a designer. The
three-discard-pile action queue is a genuine mechanism innovation --- a new
answer to the fundamental question of how to structure hand management in a
card-driven Euro. It creates planning horizons, encourages strategic
distribution, and prevents the monotony of optimal-hand-every-round play. The
shared company investment structure generates the kind of cooperative-
competitive tension that makes multi-player economic games dynamic rather than
solitary. And the parallel scoring tracks (company investment, bookkeeping,
diamond mining) ensure that no single strategy dominates, rewarding players
who can balance multiple objectives without losing focus. At its core,
Mombasa is a game about investment timing --- when to expand, when to deepen
your stake, when to diversify, and when to let others do the work for you.
The pile rotation mechanism makes that timing question feel genuinely novel
rather than a variation on well-worn Euro structures.
