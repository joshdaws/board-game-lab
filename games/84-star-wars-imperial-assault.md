## Design Notes

Designer: Justin Kemppainen, Corey Konieczka & Jonathan Ying.
Published by Fantasy Flight Games (2014).

### Theme Integration

Star Wars: Imperial Assault translates the original trilogy's central
narrative -- a small band of heroes resisting an overwhelming military
empire -- into a tactical combat system where the asymmetry between sides
is not just mechanical but emotional. The rebel players control named
heroes with unique abilities, personal story arcs, and progression systems
that make each character feel like a protagonist. The Imperial player
controls an army -- stormtroopers, probe droids, AT-STs, Vader himself --
deploying them as a faceless, replenishing force. This one-vs-many
structure does not merely model the Star Wars power dynamic; it recreates
the feeling of it. The rebels are precious and vulnerable. The Empire is
expendable and relentless. When a hero is wounded, the table feels it.
When a squad of stormtroopers is eliminated, the Imperial player deploys
more.

The campaign mode embeds individual missions within a branching narrative
that mirrors the structure of the films. Heroes gain experience and
equipment between missions, growing from scrappy rebels into powerful
operatives. The Imperial player gains influence and access to stronger
units, escalating the threat in parallel. Side missions offer detours
that can recruit iconic allies (Luke, Han, Chewbacca) or confront
legendary villains, creating the episodic adventure structure that Star
Wars storytelling demands. The campaign is not a sequence of disconnected
scenarios -- it is a story where earlier choices shape later challenges,
where a failed mission has consequences that carry forward, and where
the final confrontation feels earned because both sides have been building
toward it across sessions.

The miniatures themselves serve the theme in ways that cardboard tokens
could not. Positioning a squad of stormtroopers in a corridor, flanking
a rebel hero with elite Royal Guards, or watching an AT-ST round a corner
creates visual moments that evoke the source material. The game understands
that Star Wars is a visual property, and the physical presence of
miniatures on modular tile maps transforms tactical decisions into
cinematic tableaux.

### Core Loop

Each campaign mission follows this round structure, with rebels and the
Imperial player alternating activations:

1. ACTIVATION PHASE - Players alternate activating figures. The rebel
   players choose one hero to activate; then the Imperial player activates
   one deployment group. This continues until all figures have activated.
   Each activation consists of 2 actions chosen from:
   - MOVE - Move up to your speed value in spaces. Figures can move
     through allies freely and through enemies by spending an extra
     movement point, but cannot end movement in an occupied space.
     Terrain affects movement (doors must be opened, difficult terrain
     costs extra movement).
   - ATTACK - Choose a target within range and line of sight. Roll
     attack dice (colored by weapon type: blue for ranged accuracy,
     red/green/yellow for damage). The defender rolls white/black dice
     for defense (blocks and evade results cancel damage). Surges on
     attack dice can be spent on weapon-specific abilities (extra
     damage, pierce, stun, bleed).
   - REST - Recover strain equal to your endurance. Heroes use strain
     to trigger special abilities, creating a push-your-luck resource.
   - INTERACT - Open doors, claim objectives, search crates for items.
     Many mission objectives require interact actions on specific tiles.
   - SPECIAL - Some heroes and units have unique actions specific to
     their character card.

2. STATUS PHASE - End-of-round bookkeeping. Check mission-specific
   conditions (time limits, objective completion). Ready exhausted ability
   cards. Advance the round counter.

Between campaign missions, heroes spend experience points on class cards
(skill upgrades) and credits on item cards (equipment upgrades). The
Imperial player spends experience on Imperial class cards (ability
upgrades) and spends influence to purchase Agenda cards (special events
and side mission triggers).

The skirmish mode (competitive 2-player) replaces the campaign structure
with point-based army construction, command card decks, and symmetric
objectives on dedicated skirmish maps.

### Key Design Patterns

ONE-VS-MANY AS NARRATIVE STRUCTURE: The Imperial player is not merely an
opponent -- they are a game master who controls pacing, difficulty, and
narrative tension. This is a deliberate design choice that distinguishes
Imperial Assault from symmetric tactical combat games. The Imperial player
decides which deployment groups to activate, where to position units, and
when to deploy reinforcements. They have incomplete information about
rebel plans but complete control over the Imperial response. This
asymmetry creates emergent narrative: a clever Imperial player can
engineer moments of despair (surrounding a hero, blocking an escape route)
and moments of triumph (allowing a narrow escape that was actually a
trap). The best Imperial players understand that their role is not to
crush the rebels as efficiently as possible but to create dramatic tension
that makes victory feel earned for whichever side prevails. The one-vs-many
structure transforms tactical combat into collaborative storytelling
with adversarial mechanics.

CAMPAIGN PROGRESSION WITH XP AND CREDITS: Between missions, heroes spend
experience on class-specific upgrade cards that unlock new abilities and
modify existing ones. Credits purchase equipment from a shared market of
item cards. The Imperial player spends experience on Imperial class cards
and Agenda cards. This dual progression system creates parallel power
curves: as heroes become more capable, the Imperial threat escalates in
response. The design intent is that the game's difficulty remains
relatively constant throughout the campaign despite both sides gaining
power. In practice, the progression system creates investment and
attachment -- players care about their heroes not just because of the
character's story but because of the mechanical build they have
constructed over multiple sessions.

LINE OF SIGHT AND TACTICAL POSITIONING: Combat takes place on a grid of
square spaces, and line of sight is determined by drawing lines between
corners of the attacker's and target's spaces. If any line is unblocked,
the target is in sight. This system creates genuine tactical puzzles:
figures can use walls, doors, and terrain for cover; positioning determines
whether you can attack and whether you can be attacked. The grid movement
means that every space matters -- moving one space to the left might
block an enemy's line of sight while opening your own. This is not
abstracted area control; it is literal spatial reasoning, and the modular
tile maps ensure that every mission presents a unique tactical landscape.

THREAT LEVEL AS IMPERIAL RESOURCE GENERATION: The Imperial player
generates threat points each round (a base value that increases as the
campaign progresses). Threat is spent to deploy new groups of enemies
from the Imperial player's available deployment cards. Each deployment
card has a threat cost reflecting the unit's power. This resource system
creates a fundamental tension for the Imperial player: deploy cheap units
early to pressure the rebels immediately, or save threat for a powerful
elite unit that arrives later as a climactic challenge. The escalating
threat generation ensures that the Imperial player always has options,
even if early units are destroyed. It also means that rebel players can
never fully clear the board -- the Empire's resources are renewable, and
the rebel heroes' health is not.

DICE WITH SURGE ABILITIES: Combat resolution uses custom dice with faces
showing damage, range (for accuracy), blocks, dodges, and surges. Surges
are a wildcard result that can be spent on weapon-specific or ability-
specific effects listed on the attacker's card: extra damage, pierce
(ignore defense), stun (target loses an action), bleed (ongoing damage),
or other special effects. The surge system adds a layer of decision-making
to every attack roll. Rolling well is not just about high damage -- it is
about choosing how to allocate surge results among competing abilities.
A roll with 3 surges and low base damage might be more effective than a
roll with high base damage and no surges, depending on the available surge
abilities. This transforms dice rolling from pure randomness into a
decision space where player agency exists within stochastic outcomes.

SIDE MISSIONS AS BRANCHING CONTENT: The campaign includes a core narrative
of story missions, but between story missions, players select from
available side missions. Side missions are generated by hero-specific
personal quests, Imperial Agenda cards, and ally/villain packs. Completing
a side mission might recruit a powerful ally (Luke Skywalker joins the
rebel team) or prevent a villain from entering the Imperial roster. This
branching structure means that no two campaigns play identically -- the
side missions chosen, the outcomes of those missions, and the allies and
villains that enter play all vary. It also creates strategic decisions at
the metagame level: rebels must choose which side missions to prioritize,
knowing they cannot play them all before the campaign finale.

SKIRMISH MODE AS COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVE: Imperial Assault includes a
complete competitive 2-player skirmish mode that strips away the campaign
structure and replaces it with point-based army building, symmetric
objectives, and a command card deck that adds tactical options each round.
Skirmish uses the same miniatures, dice, and tactical movement rules but
reframes the experience as a balanced competitive game rather than an
asymmetric narrative one. This dual-mode design extends the product's
lifespan and audience: players who prefer campaign narrative and players
who prefer competitive tournament play can both find value in the same
box. The skirmish mode also served as the basis for organized play and
competitive events, creating a community ecosystem beyond the campaign
experience.

APP-DRIVEN SOLO/CO-OP CONVERSION: Fantasy Flight later released a
companion app that replaces the Imperial player with automated opponent
AI. The app controls Imperial deployments, activations, and tactical
decisions, transforming the one-vs-many game into a fully cooperative
experience. This is a significant design pivot: the app removes the
need for one player to take the adversarial role, making the game
accessible to groups where no one wants to play "the bad guy." The
app also adds narrative elements, voice acting, and scripted events
that a human Imperial player could not provide. However, the app
changes the character of the game -- the emergent drama created by
a skilled human Imperial player cannot be replicated by an algorithm.
The app is a different experience, not a strictly superior one, and
its existence highlights the design tension between accessibility and
the unique qualities of human-controlled opposition.

### What Makes It #84

Star Wars: Imperial Assault succeeds because its one-vs-many structure
does not merely create a tactical combat game -- it creates a Star Wars
story generator. The asymmetry between the desperate, precious rebel
heroes and the expendable, relentless Imperial forces produces moments
that feel authentically cinematic: a hero making a last-stand in a
corridor, an AT-ST crashing through a door, a desperate sprint to the
extraction point as stormtroopers close in. The campaign progression
transforms individual missions into episodes of a serial narrative
where investment compounds across sessions. And the tactical combat
system -- with its surge decisions, line-of-sight puzzles, and
grid-based positioning -- ensures that every activation involves
meaningful choices rather than dice-rolling spectacle. It is a game
that understands Star Wars is about the feeling of resistance against
overwhelming odds, and it builds every system to reproduce that feeling
at the table.
