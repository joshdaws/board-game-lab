## Design Notes

Designers: Raymond Edwards, Suzanne Goldberg, Gary Grady. Published by
Sleuth Publications / Space Cowboys (1981).

### Theme Integration

Sherlock Holmes Consulting Detective is one of the purest expressions of
theme-as-mechanism in board gaming. There is no board in the traditional
sense, no tokens to move, no dice to roll. Instead, the game presents
players with a case booklet -- a narrative document describing a crime
and its circumstances -- along with a map of Victorian London, a directory
of addresses, a set of newspapers from the relevant period, and a casebook
of numbered entries corresponding to locations players can visit. The theme
is not layered onto a mechanical framework; it IS the mechanical framework.
Players investigate a mystery by choosing which leads to follow, which
witnesses to interview, and which locations to visit, reading the
corresponding narrative entries and assembling evidence through discussion
and deduction. The game mechanizes the act of detective work itself.

The London map and directory are not decorative props -- they are the
game's primary decision tools. Players consult the directory to find
addresses of suspects, witnesses, and informants, then locate those
addresses on the map to visit them. The map creates a sense of London
as a lived-in space: you can trace your investigative path from Whitechapel
to Baker Street to Scotland Yard, and the geography of the city influences
which leads feel worth pursuing. A witness in distant Hampstead might
contain crucial evidence, but visiting her means spending one of your
limited investigative steps traveling across the city. The newspapers
provide ambient clues -- seemingly unrelated stories that might connect
to the case through lateral thinking. This mirrors actual detective work,
where the most important evidence is often hiding in plain sight among
irrelevant noise.

The scoring system compares players' investigation against Holmes himself.
After players declare their solution, Holmes reveals his own investigation:
typically solving the case by visiting only three or four locations through
brilliant deductive leaps. Players lose points for every lead they followed
beyond Holmes's count. This comparison is simultaneously humbling and
motivating -- you are not trying to solve the case (most groups manage
that eventually) but to solve it efficiently, with the fewest false leads
and dead ends. The design communicates that in detective fiction, the
quality of reasoning matters more than the quantity of evidence gathered.
Holmes wins not because he works harder but because he thinks more clearly.

### Core Loop

Each case follows a single continuous investigation with no rounds or turns
in the traditional sense:

1. CASE INTRODUCTION - One player reads the case introduction aloud from
   the casebook. This establishes the crime, the victim, the initial
   circumstances, and any obvious leads. Players discuss what they have
   learned and what questions need answering.

2. NEWSPAPER REVIEW - Players examine the day's newspaper (a multi-page
   document included with each case). The newspaper contains:
   a. Articles that may or may not relate to the current case.
   b. Advertisements that sometimes conceal coded messages or relevant
      business connections.
   c. Personal columns, obituaries, and society notices that provide
      background on suspects and witnesses.
   d. Players must distinguish signal from noise -- most content is
      irrelevant, but crucial clues are embedded in unexpected places.

3. LEAD SELECTION - Players collectively choose a lead to follow. This
   involves:
   a. Identifying a person, location, or organization from the case
      introduction, newspaper, or a previously visited lead.
   b. Looking up the relevant entry in the London Directory to find
      an address and corresponding casebook entry number.
   c. Some locations have entries only for specific cases; others appear
      across multiple cases (Baker Street, Scotland Yard, etc.).

4. LEAD INVESTIGATION - One player reads the casebook entry aloud. The
   entry provides narrative content: a witness testimony, a description of
   a crime scene, a conversation with an informant, or occasionally a dead
   end with no useful information. Players discuss the new evidence:
   a. What does this tell us about the crime?
   b. What new leads does this suggest?
   c. Does this contradict or confirm existing theories?

5. REPEAT STEPS 3-4 - Players continue following leads, visiting
   locations, and assembling evidence until they feel confident in their
   solution or decide to stop investigating. There is no mechanical limit
   on the number of leads followed, but each lead beyond Holmes's count
   reduces the final score.

6. SOLUTION AND SCORING - When ready, players answer the case questions
   (who committed the crime, how, why, and sometimes additional questions
   about secondary plots). Then Holmes's solution is read aloud, revealing
   his investigative path and reasoning. Scoring:
   a. Correct answers to each question earn points.
   b. Each lead followed beyond Holmes's lead count deducts 5 points.
   c. The final score indicates how close players came to matching
      Holmes's deductive brilliance.

### Key Design Patterns

OPEN INFORMATION AS DEDUCTIVE PLAYGROUND: Every piece of evidence in
Sherlock Holmes Consulting Detective is available to all players
simultaneously. The case booklet, newspapers, map, and directory are
shared resources. There are no hidden hands, no secret information, no
asymmetric knowledge. This total transparency creates a purely deductive
experience: the challenge is not acquiring information but interpreting it
correctly. Two players looking at the same witness testimony may draw
entirely different conclusions, and the ensuing discussion -- arguing about
what a clue means, not whether it exists -- is the game's primary
interaction. The design insight is that shared information creates richer
collaborative discussion than hidden information, because disagreement
stems from reasoning differences rather than information asymmetry.

LEAD COUNT AS EFFICIENCY METRIC: The scoring penalty for following more
leads than Holmes creates a meta-optimization challenge layered on top of
the mystery itself. Players are not just solving a crime; they are solving
it efficiently. Every lead followed is simultaneously an investment
(potentially revealing crucial evidence) and a cost (reducing the final
score). This creates tension between thoroughness and efficiency:
following every possible lead virtually guarantees solving the case but
results in a poor score, while following too few leads risks missing
critical evidence. The lead count metric transforms the investigation
from a simple puzzle into a risk-reward calculation, where players must
judge when they have enough evidence to commit to a solution. The design
pattern teaches that constraining resources (even in a narrative game)
creates meaningful decisions.

NEWSPAPER AS AMBIENT CLUE DELIVERY: The newspapers included with each
case are masterpieces of embedded narrative design. Each newspaper contains
dozens of articles, advertisements, and notices, only a fraction of which
relate to the current case. Players must scan the entire newspaper,
identifying potentially relevant items through lateral thinking and pattern
recognition. A shipping notice might reveal that a suspect had means of
transport. A society column might establish that two apparently unrelated
characters attended the same event. The newspaper mechanism delivers clues
without flagging them as clues, forcing players to exercise the same
discriminative judgment that real detectives use. The design lesson is
that embedding important information within a larger body of irrelevant
content creates a more authentic and challenging deductive experience
than presenting clues in isolation.

HOLMES AS BENCHMARK, NOT OPPONENT: The comparison to Holmes serves a
specific design function beyond scoring. By revealing Holmes's solution
path after players complete their investigation, the game provides a
masterclass in deductive reasoning. Holmes typically visits 3-4 locations,
each chosen through brilliant logical leaps that connect seemingly
unrelated evidence. Reading his solution is often the most rewarding
moment of the experience, as players realize which clues they overlooked
and which logical connections they failed to make. Holmes functions as an
aspirational benchmark -- not an opponent to defeat but a standard to
approach. This transforms the post-game discussion from "did we win?" to
"how could we have thought more clearly?" The design pattern is that
an expert benchmark creates a learning gradient that improves player
skill across multiple plays.

CASE BOOKLET AS BRANCHING NARRATIVE: Each case booklet contains dozens
of numbered entries, but players will only read a fraction of them during
any single investigation. The unread entries represent paths not taken --
witnesses not interviewed, locations not visited, threads not pulled. This
creates a branching narrative structure where every group's investigative
experience is unique, even though the case has a single correct solution.
The branching design means that players who replay a case (despite knowing
the solution) can explore entirely different narrative paths, discovering
new character relationships and subplots. The design principle is that
unused content is not wasted content -- it creates the possibility space
that makes each investigation feel genuine and open-ended.

COOPERATIVE DISCUSSION AS PRIMARY MECHANISM: Unlike most cooperative games
where players execute individual actions that contribute to a shared goal,
Sherlock Holmes makes group discussion the primary game mechanism. There
are no individual turns, no action points, no resource management. Players
talk. They debate theories, propose connections, argue about the
reliability of witnesses, and collectively decide which lead to follow
next. The game's quality scales directly with the quality of the
conversation. A group that listens carefully, challenges assumptions, and
synthesizes diverse perspectives will outperform a group dominated by a
single voice. The design achieves something rare: it makes conversation
itself the game, rather than something that happens alongside the game.

VICTORIAN LONDON AS DECISION SPACE: The map and directory are not
atmospheric accessories but functional decision-making tools. The map's
geography creates implicit costs for investigation: locations in different
parts of London feel distant and require commitment to visit. The directory
organizes hundreds of characters and locations alphabetically, creating a
sense of a vast, populated city where anyone might be relevant. Together,
the map and directory transform London from a setting into a decision
space -- players navigate it not by moving tokens but by reasoning about
which corner of the city might hold the next piece of the puzzle.

### What Makes It #198

Sherlock Holmes Consulting Detective is the game that proved board games
do not need boards, tokens, or dice to create deeply engaging strategic
experiences. Published in 1981, it anticipated the narrative game movement
by decades, establishing a template that games like T.I.M.E. Stories,
Detective: A Modern Crime Board Game, and countless others would follow.
Its genius lies in making deductive reasoning the game itself, not a
component of a larger mechanical system. The case booklets are some of the
finest puzzle design in the hobby, the newspapers are masterclasses in
embedded information delivery, and the Holmes benchmark transforms every
investigation into a learning experience. It endures because its core
design -- give players a mystery, tools for investigation, and the freedom
to reason their way to a solution -- is timeless, immune to the mechanical
innovations that date other games of its era.
