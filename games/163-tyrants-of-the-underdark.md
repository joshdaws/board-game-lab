# Tyrants of the Underdark

## Design Notes

Designers: Peter Lee, Rodney Thompson, Andrew Veen. Published by Gale Force Nine LLC (2016).

### Theme Integration

Tyrants of the Underdark casts players as rival drow houses vying for control
of the subterranean realms of the Forgotten Realms, the iconic Dungeons &
Dragons setting. The drow are a society defined by political treachery,
shifting alliances, and ruthless ambition — a theme that maps perfectly onto
the game's hybrid of deck building and area control. Building your deck
represents recruiting agents, assassins, priests, and monsters to your house's
cause. Placing troops on the board represents extending your political and
military influence through the Underdark's network of caverns and cities. The
two systems are not parallel tracks; they are intertwined expressions of the
same power struggle, and the tension between investing in your deck (long-term
political infrastructure) versus the board (immediate territorial control)
mirrors the drow's constant calculation between building alliances and
exploiting them.

The game's market system reinforces the theme through its half-deck
construction. Before each game, players combine two of four available
half-decks (each themed around a different drow faction: Drow, Dragons,
Elementals, Demons) to form the market. This modular construction means
the available cards — and therefore the available strategies — change with
every game. It also reflects the Underdark's shifting political landscape:
different factions rise and fall in prominence, and the savvy tyrant adapts
to whichever powers are currently ascendant. The faction half-decks are not
just thematic dressing; each emphasizes different mechanical strategies
(board control, deck thinning, direct attacks, victory point generation),
so the combination chosen fundamentally alters the game's strategic texture.

The board itself — a network of interconnected sites representing Underdark
cities and strongholds — grounds the abstract deck building in physical
territory. Controlling a site is not just a victory point marker; it provides
ongoing benefits (card draws, influence, troop deployments) and blocks
opponents from moving through your territory. The board transforms deck
building from a solitary optimization puzzle into a spatially contested
struggle where your card plays directly affect the physical map state. When
you assassinate an opponent's troop (removing it from a site), you feel the
political violence that defines drow society — it is not abstracted away
into point exchanges but enacted on the board.

### Core Loop

Each player's turn follows a straightforward sequence driven entirely by
the cards in their hand:

1. PLAY CARDS — Play all cards from your hand (typically five). Each card
   provides some combination of three resources:
   a) POWER — Used to deploy troops to the board, assassinate enemy troops,
      or activate special card abilities.
   b) INFLUENCE — Used to purchase new cards from the market row.
   c) VICTORY POINTS — Some cards provide immediate or end-game VP through
      special abilities.
   Cards may also have special abilities that trigger when played, such as
   drawing additional cards, promoting cards from your deck to your "inner
   circle" (a separate scoring pile), or manipulating the market.

2. DEPLOY TROOPS — Spend Power to place troops from your supply onto board
   sites. You can deploy to any site where you already have presence
   (troops or control) or to sites adjacent to your controlled sites.
   Initial deployment costs 1 Power per troop. Deploying to a site already
   containing enemy troops costs additional Power (you must also assassinate
   to remove them).

3. ASSASSINATE — Spend Power to remove enemy troops from the board. Each
   assassination costs Power equal to the site's defense value. Removed
   troops go to the owning player's supply, not out of the game.
   Assassination is the primary form of direct conflict, and it creates
   area majority swings that ripple across the board.

4. PURCHASE CARDS — Spend Influence to buy cards from the six-card market
   row. Purchased cards go to your discard pile (eventually shuffled into
   your draw deck). More expensive cards provide stronger effects but
   require saving Influence across multiple plays or combining Influence
   from several cards in a single hand. The market row refills immediately
   from the shared market deck.

5. PROMOTE OR DEVOUR — Some card abilities allow you to PROMOTE a card from
   your discard pile to your inner circle (a face-down pile scored at game
   end, worth the card's VP value — the card is permanently removed from
   your cycling deck, thinning it while banking points). Other abilities
   let you DEVOUR cards — remove them from your deck entirely without
   scoring, purely for deck thinning.

6. CLEANUP — Discard all played cards and remaining hand cards. Draw five
   new cards. If your draw pile is empty, shuffle your discard pile to
   form a new draw pile.

The game ends when a set number of troops from the shared supply have been
deployed (scaling by player count) or when the market deck is exhausted.
Final scoring combines: VP from controlled board sites, VP from total troops
on the board, VP from cards in your inner circle, VP from card abilities,
and bonus VP from controlling connected networks of sites.

### Key Design Patterns

DECK BUILDING MEETS AREA CONTROL AS GENRE HYBRID: Tyrants of the Underdark's
defining design choice is fusing two well-established genres — deck building
(Dominion-style card purchasing and cycling) and area control (El Grande-style
majority competition on a shared map). Neither system is subordinate; both are
fully developed and deeply interconnected. Cards generate the resources to
contest the board; board positions provide ongoing advantages that improve your
card play. This creates a strategic dilemma that neither genre produces alone:
should you invest Influence in powerful cards that will improve future turns,
or spend Power deploying troops to lock down board positions before opponents
do? The hybrid structure means players who focus exclusively on deck
optimization will lose the board, while players who ignore deck quality will
run out of fuel. The sweet spot is dynamic and opponent-dependent, which gives
the game strategic depth unusual for its 2.55 weight class.

HALF-DECK MARKET CONSTRUCTION AS VARIABLE STRATEGY SPACE: Before each game,
players select two of four faction half-decks to shuffle together as the
market. Each half-deck contains approximately 40 cards with distinct strategic
personalities: one faction emphasizes troop deployment and board control,
another focuses on deck thinning and engine efficiency, a third provides
direct attack and disruption, and the fourth offers victory point generation
and inner circle promotion. The combination of two half-decks determines which
strategies are available and effective. Pairing the aggressive faction with the
board-control faction produces a combat-heavy game; pairing the engine faction
with the VP faction creates a more contemplative optimization race. This
modular market system provides substantial replayability from a modest card
pool and teaches players to read the available strategy space before committing
to a plan — a meta-skill that transfers across deck-building games.

INNER CIRCLE AS DECK-THINNING REWARD: The promote mechanic allows players to
move cards from their discard pile to a permanent scoring zone called the
inner circle. Promoted cards are worth their VP value at game end but are
permanently removed from the cycling deck. This creates a dual incentive:
banking victory points AND improving deck quality by removing weaker cards.
The strategic tension is in timing — promoting a card too early removes a
useful tool from your deck, but promoting too late means the card clogs your
draws during the critical endgame. The inner circle also creates a hidden
scoring dimension: opponents can see your board position but cannot easily
track your inner circle's contents, introducing partial hidden information
into an otherwise open-state game.

NETWORK BONUS AS SPATIAL PLANNING INCENTIVE: At game end, players score
bonus VP for the largest connected group of sites they control. This
incentivizes building contiguous territory rather than scattershot
deployments, and it creates a geometric puzzle overlaid on the area-control
competition. A player might sacrifice a strategically valuable isolated site
to reinforce a connection between two controlled clusters, merging them into
a single high-scoring network. The network bonus also makes assassination
strategically nuanced: removing an enemy troop from a connecting site can
split their network in two, costing them significant endgame points. Territory
is not just about quantity but about topology.

TAKE THAT AS POLITICAL INTERACTION: The assassination mechanic provides
direct, targeted conflict — you spend Power to remove a specific opponent's
troop from a specific site. This is not indirect competition (drafting a card
someone else wanted) but explicit aggression. The Take That element is
thematically appropriate for a drow power struggle and mechanically important
for preventing runaway leaders. A player who builds a dominant board position
becomes a target for collective opposition, as other players recognize the
threat and spend Power dismantling their network. The direct conflict also
creates negotiation opportunities ("If you attack their eastern flank, I
will not contest your western sites"), though the game includes no formal
alliance mechanics — alliances are emergent and unreliable, as befits the
drow setting.

DELAYED PURCHASE AS MARKET TIMING: Purchased cards go to the discard pile,
not the hand, meaning they will not be available until the deck cycles.
This delay creates a planning horizon: cards bought early will cycle through
your deck many times; cards bought late may only appear once or twice before
the game ends. Experienced players evaluate market cards not just by their
raw power but by their timing value — a strong card bought in the final
rounds is effectively worthless. The delayed purchase mechanic also interacts
with deck thinning: the more aggressively you promote and devour cards, the
faster your deck cycles, and the sooner new purchases arrive in your hand.
Deck velocity becomes a strategic variable that rewards players who think
about their deck as a system rather than a collection.

### What Makes It #163

Tyrants of the Underdark succeeds by solving the genre-hybrid problem that
many designs stumble on: making both halves of the hybrid feel essential
rather than tacked on. The deck building is not a card-draw minigame bolted
onto an area-control board — it is the engine that powers your territorial
ambitions. The area control is not a scoring track that happens to use a
map — it is a contested space where your card investments materialize as
physical power. The half-deck market system gives the game structural
variety that most deck builders achieve only through expansions, and the
inner circle promotion mechanic elegantly combines scoring with deck
thinning. At 60 minutes and 2.55 weight, it occupies a sweet spot between
the casual deck builder and the sprawling area-control epic, delivering
genuine strategic depth in a package that remains accessible to players
familiar with either parent genre.
