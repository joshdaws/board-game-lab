## Design Notes

Designer: Randy Flynn. Published by Flatout Games / AEG (2021).

### Theme Integration

Cascadia draws its identity from the Pacific Northwest ecosystem, and the theme is
not merely cosmetic. The five wildlife species, bear, elk, salmon, hawk, and fox,
each score according to patterns that echo their real behavioral tendencies. Hawks
score for isolation because raptors are territorial. Salmon score for runs because
they migrate in linear paths. Bears score for pairs because they are often seen with
cubs. The connection between animal behavior and scoring pattern is loose enough to
allow mechanical elegance but tight enough that players internalize the scoring rules
through thematic association. This is theme as mnemonic device: the nature metaphor
makes the game easier to learn and remember.

The habitat tiles reinforce this integration. Each tile depicts one or two terrain
types from the Cascadian landscape, mountains, forests, prairies, wetlands, and
rivers, and displays icons indicating which wildlife species may be placed there.
Building your personal landscape is not abstract optimization; it looks and feels
like assembling a functioning ecosystem. The largest contiguous group of each terrain
type scores a bonus, encouraging players to think about landscape continuity in ways
that mirror ecological connectivity. The visual result of a completed game is a
patchwork wilderness that tells a spatial story.

The nature theme also does critical accessibility work. Board games about economic
engines or military conflict carry genre baggage that limits their audience. A game
about building a beautiful landscape populated with wildlife carries almost no
thematic barrier. Flynn and the Flatout Games team understood that the Pacific
Northwest setting is not just pleasant; it is strategically non-threatening, inviting
players who might otherwise never engage with spatial optimization puzzles.

### Core Loop

Cascadia's turn structure is elemental in its simplicity, with depth emerging from
the interaction between two simultaneous placement decisions:

1. DRAFT A PAIR - The central display shows four habitat tiles, each paired with a
   wildlife token drawn randomly from the bag. On your turn, you select one pair,
   taking both the tile and its associated token. The pairing is the game's central
   tension generator: the tile you want is often paired with a token you do not need,
   and vice versa.

2. PLACE THE HABITAT TILE - Add the drafted tile to your personal landscape, adjacent
   to at least one existing tile. Terrain type continuity matters for end-game scoring
   (largest contiguous group of each type earns a bonus), so placement is a long-term
   spatial planning exercise. Each tile shows one or two terrain types, creating
   natural borders and corridors in your landscape.

3. PLACE THE WILDLIFE TOKEN - Place the drafted wildlife token on any tile in your
   landscape that displays a matching wildlife icon and does not already have a token.
   Token placement is governed by the current game's wildlife scoring cards, which
   define the spatial patterns each species needs to maximize points. Placement is
   permanent; tokens cannot be moved once placed.

4. KEYSTONE ACTIVATION - If you place a token on a tile that shows only a single
   wildlife icon (a "keystone" tile), you earn a nature token. Nature tokens can be
   spent on future turns to replace any wildlife tokens in the display, drawing new
   ones from the bag, or to take a tile and token from different positions in the
   display rather than taking a matched pair. Keystones are rare and valuable,
   creating micro-economies of flexibility.

5. REFILL - Slide the display to fill gaps and draw new tiles and tokens to restore
   four pairs. If three or more tokens in the display show the same wildlife type,
   all tokens are wiped and redrawn (preventing stagnation).

### Key Design Patterns

DUAL-LAYER SCORING: Cascadia's scoring operates on two independent but spatially
entangled layers. Habitat tiles score for contiguous terrain groups (largest group of
each type earns a bonus). Wildlife tokens score for specific spatial patterns
dictated by scoring cards. These two systems compete for the same physical space on
your landscape. A tile placement that extends your mountain range might not be the
optimal location for the bear token you need to complete a pair. This dual-layer
architecture means every placement decision requires balancing two different
optimization problems simultaneously, generating depth that far exceeds the game's
1.84 weight rating.

LINKED DRAFTING: The decision to pair tiles with tokens in the display is the
engine that drives Cascadia's decision space. In most drafting games, you choose a
single item from a shared pool. Here, you must evaluate a two-dimensional matrix:
each of four tiles is linked to a wildlife token, and you are evaluating both
simultaneously. The pairing is random, which means the display sometimes offers
perfect combinations and sometimes forces painful compromises. This randomized
linkage creates decision tension without requiring complex rules. The mechanism is
invisible to new players (they just "pick a pair") but deeply strategic for
experienced ones.

WILDLIFE SCORING CARD VARIABILITY: Each of the five wildlife species has multiple
scoring cards (typically A, B, C, and sometimes D variants), and only one card per
species is active in any given game. This creates massive combinatorial replayability.
The A cards tend to be simpler patterns (pairs, lines), while higher-letter cards
introduce more complex spatial requirements. The scoring card selection also
fundamentally changes strategic priorities: a game with salmon scoring for long runs
plays very differently from one where salmon score for adjacency to other species.
This variability system is a replayability engine that costs almost nothing in
component overhead.

NATURE TOKEN ECONOMY: Keystone tiles, those showing only a single wildlife icon,
reward players with nature tokens that serve as a flexible currency for breaking the
display's constraints. You can spend a token to decouple a tile from its paired
wildlife token, drafting from different positions in the display. This creates a
micro-economy of flexibility: keystone tiles are often suboptimal for habitat
building (single icon means fewer placement options for wildlife), but the nature
token they generate provides future optionality. The trade-off between immediate
spatial value and future flexibility is a clean, elegant decision.

ACCESSIBILITY THROUGH VISUAL CLARITY: Cascadia's component design is a case study
in visual accessibility. Terrain types are distinguished by both color and art style.
Wildlife tokens are chunky, tactile discs with clear animal silhouettes. The scoring
cards use large, unambiguous diagrams. The personal landscape you build is visually
readable from across the table. This clarity is not incidental; it is a design
choice that reduces cognitive load and makes the game approachable for players of
all ages and experience levels. Flynn understood that a puzzle game lives or dies on
whether players can see the puzzle.

EMERGENT SPATIAL TENSION: Although Cascadia is technically a multiplayer solitaire
game (players build separate landscapes), interaction emerges through the shared
display. Drafting a pair removes it from availability for other players, creating
indirect competition. More subtly, observing opponents' landscapes reveals what they
need, which informs your drafting priorities. The game avoids direct conflict, which
suits its nature theme, but creates meaningful interaction through the shared economy
of tiles and tokens.

SCALABLE COMPLEXITY: The base game ships with three difficulty levels of wildlife
scoring cards, and the milestone and scenario systems add further challenge layers.
This scalable complexity means the game grows with its audience. A family playing
with A-level scoring cards experiences a gentle, satisfying puzzle. Experienced
gamers using C-level cards face a brutally tight spatial optimization challenge.
The core rules never change; only the scoring conditions scale. This is the gold
standard for weight-adjustable design.

ANTI-HOARDING DISPLAY WIPE: The rule that three or more matching wildlife tokens in
the display triggers a complete token refresh is a small but critical anti-degenerate
mechanism. Without it, certain token types could accumulate in the display, starving
players who need them. The wipe ensures that the token pool remains diverse, preventing
the display from calcifying into unusable configurations. It is an invisible guardrail
that most players never consciously notice.

### What Makes It #57

Cascadia is the rare game that achieves genuine depth at ultra-low complexity. Its
1.84 weight rating places it firmly in gateway territory, yet experienced gamers find
rich spatial optimization puzzles in its dual-layer scoring system. The linked
drafting mechanism generates decision tension with a single, intuitive action. The
wildlife scoring card variability ensures no two games present the same optimization
challenge. And the Pacific Northwest theme does triple duty: it provides thematic
coherence, aids learnability through behavioral associations, and removes genre
barriers to entry. Flynn designed a game that is as satisfying for a ten-year-old
building a pretty landscape as it is for a seasoned gamer min-maxing hawk territories,
and that range of engagement within a single, simple system is Cascadia's defining
achievement.
